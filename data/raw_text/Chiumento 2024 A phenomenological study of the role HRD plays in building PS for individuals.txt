                                             Abstract
   A Phenomenological Study of the Role Human Resources Development Plays in Building

   Psychological Safety for Individuals with Cognitive Deficits to Disclose in the Workplace

                            Katherine Chiumento M.S., CCC-SLP, Ed.D.
                                  Drexel University, October 2024
                                  Chairperson: Harriette Rasmussen

       This phenomenological study examines the role of human resource (HR) professionals in

fostering a psychologically safe workplace that encourages individuals with cognitive deficits to

disclose their diagnoses and seek accommodations. Guided by three key research questions—1)

How do HR professionals define and operationalize psychological safety in the workplace? 2)

How do HR professionals incorporate the needs of employees with cognitive deficits into their

psychological safety practices? 3) How do HR professionals describe the inclusion of employees

with cognitive deficits?—the study gathered insights from HR professionals with experience in

managing invisible disabilities. The first theme identified was the facilitation of psychological

safety for all employees, with many participants emphasizing the importance of progressive

leadership to achieve organizational climate and culture goals, a theme highlighted in the second

finding. The third theme revealed how participants’ personal experiences in HR shape their

ability to bridge the gap between leadership and employees, all while upholding their own ethical

standards. These findings offer valuable perspectives on how HR professionals navigate the

challenges of promoting an inclusive work environment for individuals with cognitive

impairments.

       Keywords: Invisible Disabilities, Psychological Safety, DEI, Accommodations, Human

Resources

                            Chapter 1: Introduction to the Research

       In today's diverse and dynamic work environments, the need for belonging has emerged

as a critical factor in fostering employee engagement, well-being, and overall organizational

success. Belonging, a core component of psychological safety, is the sense that one is accepted,

valued, and included within a team or organization (Maslow, 1943). Research has consistently

shown that employees who feel a strong sense of belonging are more engaged, productive, and

loyal to their employers (Jena et al., 2018). For individuals with visible disabilities, such as

mobility impairments, belonging is often more straightforward to achieve through physical

accommodations and social inclusion. However, for those with invisible disabilities—such as

cognitive deficits, mental health conditions, or chronic illnesses—achieving a sense of belonging

can be more challenging, as these employees may face discrimination, misunderstanding, or a

lack of support in the workplace (Dong and Mamboleo, 2023).

       Individuals with invisible disabilities often struggle with the stigma and misconceptions

surrounding their conditions, which can prevent them from fully participating in the work

environment or disclosing their needs (Dong and Mamboleo, 2023). As a result, they may

experience isolation or anxiety, fearing judgment or a lack of empathy from colleagues and

supervisors (Vogus & Taylor, 2018). This exclusion can significantly impact their psychological

safety and overall well-being, undermining their ability to perform at their best. Organizations

that fail to address the unique challenges faced by individuals with invisible disabilities may

inadvertently create environments where these employees do not feel safe to express their true

selves or ask for necessary accommodations. To truly support all employees, organizations must

go beyond token representation in diversity programs and focus on creating inclusive cultures

that actively foster belonging for individuals with invisible disabilities. This requires thoughtful

                                                                                                      2


strategies, such as open dialogue, tailored accommodations, and a commitment to educating all

staff on the experiences of those with cognitive and mental health challenges.

       Psychological safety is a person’s perception that they can engage in voicing behaviors

such as making mistakes, asking questions, or pointing out concerns without fear of humiliation

or punishment (Edmondson, 2018). Dong and Mamboleo (2023) identified low levels of

psychological safety in the workplace as one of the primary reasons individuals with invisible

disabilities leave their positions, and Bessing et al., 2022 argued that psychological safety is

required for individuals to request workplace accommodations. Rather than risk humiliation or

even retaliation from leadership by requesting accommodations, individuals with invisible

disabilities are resigning and risking declined independence in their daily lives (Rumrill, 2021).

Introduction to Human Resources Role

       In recent research within human resource development (HRD), the connection between

employee engagement, trust, and psychological well-being has been emphasized as critical for

building strong organizational cultures (Cooke, 2022; Jena et al., 2018). Jena et al. (2018)

highlighted how psychological well-being, including feelings of safety and belonging, plays a

central role in fostering employee trust and engagement. This aligns with the broader framework

of Human Centered Human Resources Development (HCHRD), which focuses on developing

employees as whole individuals and investing in their capabilities and well-being (Cooke et al.,

2022). When employees feel psychologically safe and supported, their engagement increases,

contributing to higher productivity and lower attrition rates. Transparent leadership is crucial in

this process, as it reduces frustration and anxiety, leading to a more focused and trusting work

environment (Jena et al., 2018; Jiang et al., 2018). Thus, HRD professionals bear significant

                                                                                                     3


responsibility in cultivating a culture of trust and psychological safety through thoughtful

leadership and initiatives that support the well-being of all employees.

       One of the critical challenges in HRD today is ensuring that DEI (Diversity, Equity, and

Inclusion) initiatives fully represent individuals with invisible disabilities, such as those with

cognitive deficits or mental health conditions. Research by Zurn et al. (2022) and others (e.g.,

Hyensi et al., 2023) has shown that while physical disabilities are often more easily

accommodated in the workplace, individuals with cognitive or mental health disabilities face

greater barriers, including discrimination and a lack of support. To address this, organizations are

increasingly focusing on including individuals with invisible disabilities in DEI initiatives, not

only through representation but also through structural actions that go beyond awareness and

education (Gupta & Priyadarshi, 2020). HRD professionals are crucial in bridging this gap by

fostering inclusive training programs and collaborating with healthcare experts to better

understand the needs of individuals with invisible disabilities, ensuring that these employees are

not only represented but also given the support and accommodations they require to thrive in the

workplace.

Difficulties in the Workplace for Individuals with Invisible Disabilities

       This research utilized Multiple Sclerosis (MS) as a relevant example of an invisible

disability; MS is the most common progressive neurologic disease of young adults worldwide

(Ghasemi et al., 2017). The average age of diagnosis of MS is 20-40 years old (Wallin, 2019),

which falls directly in line with the “prime working age” as defined by the Bureau of Labor

Statistics (United States Census Bureau, 2023). Individuals with MS face the challenge of

physical exhaustion to navigate the workspace. While there are ways to aid in maintaining

physical abilities, these types of accommodations can be time-consuming, expensive, and not

                                                                                                    4


always specialized to target an individual’s needs. People with MS (PwMS) are often prescribed

therapeutic interventions such as physical, occupational, and speech therapy. These therapies

can address physical symptoms such as difficulties with balance, walking, hand grip, articulation

of speech, and swallowing. However, they do not address a key symptom found in Multiple

Sclerosis: cognitive deficits. In fact, individuals with MS cite cognitive decline as the primary

reason for leaving or decreasing their employment (Rumrill et al., 2021).

Cognitive Concerns

       There are two primary impacts of MS related to cognition: episodic memory and

processing speed (Sumowski et al., 2018). Episodic memory is defined as “the conscious

recollection of a personal experience that contains information on what has happened and also

where and when it happened” (Pause et al., 2013, p. 1), while processing speed refers to “the rate

at which relatively simple perceptual and automatic cognitive operations can be carried out”

(Yumba, 2017, p. 1). Deficits in processing speed and episodic memory can make carrying out

professional tasks difficult, posing challenges for employees who need to work and for their

employers who need functional employees.

       As is the case with physical manifestations of MS, PwMS can work with therapists to

mitigate the difficulties of cognitive deficits. However, cognitive therapy is not always covered

by insurance; it can be costly and time-consuming. Also, therapists may not be able to assist with

specific work-related concerns. Depending on the training and experience of the cognitive

therapist, they may focus on broader skills. While cognitive therapists may be able to target

overall skills like attention, memory, problem-solving, and planning, they may not be able to

work directly with an individual on specific issues related to the workplace; not every concern

                                                                                                     5


can be replicated or simulated in a therapy session. This is when accommodations come into play

in the workplace.

Workplace Accommodations

       In 1990, the first Americans with Disability Act was signed into effect. One of the many

rights it put in place was for the requirement of reasonable accommodations in the workplace for

any person considered disabled. According to the Americans with Disabilities Act a reasonable

accommodation is

       any change to the application or hiring process, to the job, to the way the job is done, or

       the work environment that allows a person with a disability who is qualified for the job to

       perform the essential functions of that job and enjoy equal employment opportunities.

       Accommodations are considered ‘reasonable’ if they do not create an undue hardship or a

       direct threat. (ADA Network, 2018)

       In order to receive an accommodation, an individual needs to not only disclose their

disability but also provide proof of the disability from a medical professional. Thus,

accommodations are only an option if and when an individual discloses their disability. Research

shows that not everyone feels comfortable and may choose not to make that type of disclosure to

their employers (Dong & Mamboleo, 2023), whether from fear of discrimination, lack of

understanding, or any other myriads of reasons. Even if the individual is participating in

therapeutic services to address their cognitive issues, the necessary suggested environmental and

behavioral changes in the workplace may not be an option to them without disclosure. So why

are PwMS leaving their positions rather than disclosing their disability to receive

accommodations?

                                        The Problem Statement

                                                                                                      6


       Human resources personnel play a key role in the establishment of psychological safety

in the workplace (Ge, 2020), but the role of human resources management in fostering

psychological safety as relates to disability disclosure is understudied. Human resources

departments are also primarily responsible for diversity, equity, and inclusion (DEI) initiatives.

With increased awareness in the workplace of the various aspects of an individual’s identity in

terms of race, sexuality, gender, ethnicity, nationality, and religious background, one intersection

may be missing from diversity, equity, and inclusion (DEI) initiatives and psychological safety

considerations in the workplace: disability. This study will also investigate the effects of DEI

initiatives in professional settings on individuals with invisible disabilities to determine how, if at

all, the level of representation of disabilities in DEI initiatives contributes to psychological safety

in the workplace.

       Employees with invisible disabilities feel forced to leave their positions due to physical

and cognitive fatigue related to the traditional office environment and culture (Rumrill et al.,

2021) rather than disclose their disability to their employers. A decrease in employment status

can be emotionally and financially debilitating to an individual who still wants and needs to

participate in a professional capacity (Conradson et al., 2020).

       Individuals with invisible disabilities such as MS have unique needs in the workplace.

PwMS who can access accommodations to lower physical and cognitive fatigue are more likely

to maintain their employment status, a major contributor to the quality of life for individuals who

still want and need to participate in the workforce (Doogan & Playford, 2014). Too many PwMS,

however, are instead choosing to decrease or abandon their employment status rather than

disclose their disability based on the perceived low levels of psychological safety in the

                                                                                                    7


workplace (Bessing et al., 2022). When we have the solution right in front of us, it is important

to ask what is really going on.

                                  Purpose and Significance of the Problem

Purpose Statement

       The purpose of this study was to understand the role of human resources departments in

creating a psychologically safe work environment in which individuals with invisible disabilities

feel able to disclose their disability to their employers so that they can maintain employment and

independence. The results of this study intend to inform practices of human resources

departments while supporting the needs of individuals with invisible disabilities such as MS to

remain in the workforce.

Significance of the Problem

       Multiple sclerosis (MS) is the most common progressive neurologic disease of young

adults worldwide (Ghasemi et al., 2017). It may sound like a death sentence, but for the nearly

one million individuals estimated to be diagnosed with MS in the United States,1 it is their new

normal. Though no two individuals with MS “look-alike” in terms of symptom presentation,

there are common indications of the disease: increased fatigue, nerve pain, exhaustion (different

from fatigue), decreased balance, foot drag, numbness in extremities, and decreased fine motor

skills (Ghasemi et al., 2017). People with MS (PwMS) also report difficulty with coordination of

swallowing, quality and intensity of vocal quality, as well as difficulty with cognitive skills such

as memory, problem-solving, and executive function skills (Ghasemi et al., 2017). PwMS also




1
 This number is based largely on revisions of estimates from older data (Wallin, 2019) and it is
believed to be even greater.

                                                                                                     8


present with depression or anxiety diagnoses (Ghasemi et al., 2017). As unique as the symptom

presentation can be, the numbers of those diagnosed with MS are growing.

       There is no known cure for MS (Valladolid, 2023). While it is no longer a “death

sentence,” it currently is a life sentence. There are medications to mitigate symptom

presentation; however, these do not work for everyone and can provide their own set of side

effects. Overall, daily life is difficult for individuals with MS. In itself, MS can make finding and

maintaining employment a challenge.

       The average age of diagnosis for a PwMS occurs between the ages of 20-40 years old, so

it is imperative to understand how to serve this population to set them up for success early on in

their careers. MS has no known cure and presents differently for each individual, but it is

estimated that 80% of PwMS will demonstrate cognitive decline due to their diagnosis (“MS

Society,” n.d.).

       PwMS continue to face many obstacles in the workplace based on their symptom

presentation. In a recent meta-analysis, Gerhard et al. (2020) identified the role of cognitive

fatigue in office absenteeism. Cognitive fatigue is a form of active fatigue when an individual

feels exhausted from overexerting themself performing cognitive tasks (Pickering et al., 2023).

Often, PwMS are instructed by their care team to “take breaks” and to “rest often” (MS Society,

n.d.) However, for individuals with no accommodations in place, these breaks can lead to

absenteeism, as Gerhard et al. (2020) referenced in their meta-analysis. Gerhard et al. (2020)

found that individuals who did not have accommodations in place reported increased negative

work experiences than individuals who did have the accommodations in place. With proper job

accommodations in place, this does not have to be the outcome. As one example, Jaworski et al.

(2021) found that by utilizing accommodations in the workplace, PwMS were able to decrease

                                                                                                     9


“deteriorating work status” (p. 25) through diagnosis disclosure. In this study, the individuals

who felt “safe” to disclose their diagnosis reported high levels of “conscientiousness” on self-

report surveys, described as “one’s proclivity for deliberation, achievement, and order” (Jaworski

et al., 2021, p 1125). It is important to note that in this scenario, the onus was on the individual to

seek accommodation, as opposed to institutional initiative.

       Another primary symptom of MS is depression, which can decrease an individual’s

ability to be conscientious. Depression is defined as “a mood disorder that causes a persistent

feeling of sadness and loss of interest” (American Psychiatric Association, 2013, p. 155). It is

also described as a “disruptive” disorder (American Psychiatric Association, 2013). Depression

can make following routines and completing daily tasks difficult, if not impossible. Depression

can also affect motivation and socialization, negatively impacting workplace interactions

(American Psychiatric Association, 2013).

       Another cognitive condition that manifests in MS is a deficit in attention. Attention

deficits cause difficulty in transitions, processing information, and attending to tasks. PwMS who

demonstrate attention deficits report difficulty following directions, participating in

conversational discourse, and recalling information presented verbally (DeLuca et al., 2020).

PwMS also report difficulties with visual tasks and typically complete visually-based testing that

targets attention skills with decreased accuracy and speed when compared to their age-matched

peers (DeLuca et al., 2020). In DeLuca et al.’s (2020) study, psychological safety was found to

be instrumental to workplace success of individuals with attention deficits. McIntosh et al.

(2023) found that proactive disclosure for attention deficits at work was positively correlated

with psychological safety. Proactive disclosure occurs when disclosure of a diagnosis occurs

prior to an incident/exacerbation of symptoms (McIntosh et al., 2023).

                                                                                                      10


       A goal of this study was to enable proactive disclosure for individuals with MS through

enhanced psychological safety. The researcher aimed to understand how the climate and culture

in professional environments either facilitate or destroy psychological safety. Despite research

calling for increased psychological safety, there is little evidence that anything is being done in

ways that provide support for PwMs. The unemployment rate for PwMS remains at 80%

(Jaworski et al., 2020). This research sought to determine the organizational contributors to

psychological safety to promote proactive disclosure as a route to improve conditions for

individuals with cognitive deficits and other invisible disabilities.

       This study was intended to help employers to better understand the needs of their

employees with neurodegenerative diseases, including but not limited to MS, e.g., Parkinson’s

Disease, Amyotrophic Lateral Sclerosis (ALS), and Alzheimer’s Disease. This study sought to

aid individuals in human resources departments to better understand the role of psychological

safety as it relates to accommodations but also as a service to all stakeholders in an organization.

This research aimed to increase awareness of a particular blind spot in human resources

departments so that professionals in the field can identify actionable changes. Ultimately, this

study hoped to benefit all individuals with neurodegenerative diseases who wish to maintain

their independence through employment status.

                                           Conceptual Framework

Researcher’s Stance

       The belief system of the individual researcher brings a certain worldview when

interpreting data. As a speech and language pathologist (SLP), the researcher finds herself

aligning with a constructivist researcher stance. She believes reality is based on the

interpretation of people’s different experiences and that multiple realities exist. This worldview

aligns with the practice of cognitive therapy, in which individuals use language and cultural

differences to interpret reality. Different people may have different interpretations and

experiences of the same scenarios, thus creating multiple realities that exist at once. It is these

multiple realities that provided depth of information for the study, allowing the researcher to

distill the essence of the phenomenon that was examined. In this study, the use of interviews

provided a better understanding of the psychological safety in the workplace required for

individuals to disclose diagnoses with cognitive deficits.

       While the primary epistemology and ontology of this research were driven by the social

constructivist view, they were also guided by principles from critical disability theory. This is the

understanding that disability is a social construct, dependent on how a person differs from the

normed “abled” society (Wooden & Surface, 2023). Framing disability as a societal construct

was paramount to the foundation of this study. While one can understand there are systems of

oppression in the workplace, not everyone is affected by them equally, even individuals

considered within the same population. This understanding speaks to the nature of qualitative

research. Though researchers endeavor to determine a “phenomenon” that unites the experience

                                                                                                    12


of the participants, there will always be discrepancies and differences among the population

being studied. This is why taking a constructivist stance was suitable for this research; it

acknowledges the multiple realities that exist within shared experiences.

Experiential Base

       As a Speech and Language Pathologist who provided cognitive therapy almost

exclusively to PwMS, the researcher had seen firsthand the benefits of providing reasonable

accommodations in the workplace. She worked with individuals who were fighting to stay

independent through maintaining their employment but struggled with the unique problems they

face at work. She and the patients worked together to problem-solve and implement strategies to

help manage their cognitive deficits in the workplace. The researcher educated her patients on

their rights to accommodations in the workplace and encouraged the patients to be their own best

advocates. However, this researcher has observed that no amount of education can help patients

if they feel unsafe in their workplace.

       The researcher’s professional experience as an SLP included patients who disclosed their

diagnosis with their employers and patients who did not. When patients disclosed their

disabilities in the workplace, they were able to work through problems and concerns with the

knowledge that they would be able to integrate the solutions into their daily lives, including their

professional ones. When a patient did not disclose in the workplace, it was difficult to identify

sustainable and helpful solutions to their cognitive difficulties in the workplace, as this required

utilizing accommodations provided by their employer.

       On an even more personal note, the researcher can remember the fear of having to

disclose her own diagnosis with her employers at the start of the COVID-19 pandemic. At the

time, she was considered to have an underlying medical condition associated with a higher risk

                                                                                                  13


of infection. Even as an individual working in an outpatient rehabilitation clinic, she was

concerned that her diagnosis might keep her from being considered for promotions. The

researcher feared that by disclosing her diagnosis, she would have changed how her employers

viewed her as an employee. Even after disclosing this, she continued to worry her employers

would see her as a liability or potentially less productive than her colleagues. She was concerned

with how this would affect her future with the organization.

Researcher Organization of the Literature Review

       Using a qualitative approach, the study sought to identify the phenomenon of

psychological safety in the workplace as it relates to individuals with cognitive deficits. The

literature used to frame this study included (1) the experiences of PwMS in the workplace and

current concerns and perceived barriers to maintaining their current level of employment; (2) the

roles of empathy and psychological safety in the workplace; (3) and the evolving roles of the

HRD professional in the workplace.

       The literature review begins with the experiences of employees with invisible disabilities,

particularly MS, in a traditional workplace to better understand the specific difficulties they face

and situate this study within the current knowledge base. The reviewed research then zooms out

to discuss the role of empathy and psychological safety in the workplace, as PwMS have cited a

lack of psychological safety as a primary reason for leaving their workplace (Dong et al., 2023).

       The second stream examines the locus of responsibility for psychological safety in the

workplace as a broad and general idea before distilling down to the more specific concepts being

focused on in this research. The research questions examined the role of psychological safety in

disclosing disabilities. Thus, a review of psychological safety as a phenomenon became

                                                                                                   14


foundational to understanding what is or is not happening in environments where people are not

disclosing a cognitive disability diagnosis.

       The final stream then focuses on the role of the HRD professional as a supporter of the

employee in the workplace and HRD theories that demonstrate the HRD professional’s place in

an organization to build and facilitate an open environment in the organization. It also

demonstrated how organizations integrate disabilities with cognitive deficits into DEI strategies.

Oftentimes, when discussing DEI initiatives, the average employee/employer thinks of race,

ethnicity, gender, and sexuality. What may not be considered is a person’s “ableness,” especially

with an invisible disability such as MS. One component of this research considered the role of

DEI initiatives and strategies in different organizations in addressing cognitive deficits and

diagnoses.

                                           Definition of Terms

Cognitive Deficits

        Cognitive deficits are impairments in areas of mental processes such as attention/focus,

immediate and delayed memories, problem-solving, reasoning, and pragmatic skills. These skills

define how people take in information, encode information, and utilize information (Sumowski

et al., 2018).

Cognitive Therapy

        This is a form of therapeutic intervention provided for individuals with cognitive deficits

to increase skills such as immediate/delayed memory recall, attention/focus, thought

processing/organization, and problem solving. Cognitive therapy is provided by a certified

therapist, such as a Speech and Language Pathologist or Occupational Therapist (Sumowski et

al., 2018).

Diversity, Equity, and Inclusion (DEI)

                                                                                                    16


       Diversity, equity, and inclusion represent initiatives that focus on respect, value, and

belonging for all individuals in an organization, while also “addressing the unique barriers that

disadvantage a subset” of people (McCleary-Gaddy, 2019, p. 1443).

Employment Status

       Employment status refers to the level of an individual’s employment, such as full-time,

part-time, or seasonal (Conradsson et al., 2020).

Human Resources Development (HRD)

       Human resources development refers to activities that are required to develop an effective

workforce. These activities include but are not limited to training, career development,

performance development, mentoring, coaching, and tuition assistance (Frye, 2020).

Multiple Sclerosis (MS)

       MS is defined as an immune-mediated neurodegenerative disease that affects the central

nervous system. A person’s immune system reacts abnormally and causes inflammation that

damages myelin (protective substance of nerve fibers) and the nerve fibers. When damaged,

these nerve fibers send off altered or ineffective communication within the body. This can result

in physical as well as cognitive deficits in a person. It presents differently in all persons but

there are four disease courses: Clinically Isolated Syndrome, Relapsing Remitting, Secondary

Progressive, and Primary Progressive (MS Society, Definition of MS, 2022).

Neurodegenerative Disease

        Neurodegenerative disease is a disease type where the cells of the central nervous system

progressively stop working or die. Typically, these diseases become worse over time, and there

are no cures. Types of neurodegenerative diseases are MS, Parkinson’s Disease, dementia, such

                                                                                                    17


as Alzheimer’s Type, and Amyotrophic Lateral Sclerosis, also referred to as Lou Gehrig’s

Disease (MS Society, Definition of MS, 2022).

Psychological Safety

       This is a shared expectation in an organization that individuals will not be penalized,

bullied, harassed, or retaliated against for speaking up, sharing opinions, raising questions, or

making mistakes (Cummings & Worley, 2019).

                                      Assumptions and Limitations

Assumptions

       Data obtained for this research was taken from interviews of human resource (HR)

professionals working in traditional professional environments. It was assumed that individuals

were assumed to be honest and truthful to the personal experiences of those interviewed. This

research also relied on the assumption that the HR professionals interviewed recognized the

importance of a psychologically safe environment in their workplace and sought to create such

environments. In addition, this research assumed that HR professionals have the power to effect

change through their actions in the workplace to foster psychologically safe environments. This

research also assumed that inclusion in the workplace is desirable to increase productivity.

Finally, it assumed that a psychologically safe environment will create an inclusive workplace.

Limitations

       The researcher is a speech and language pathologist providing cognitive therapy to

PwMS in a private practice. As such, she may have been biased towards the benefits of

disclosure and accommodations for disabilities with cognitive deficits. Also, as this was a

phenomenological study, the sample size is a limitation. It may not be transferable to all

organizations. The need to maintain confidentiality was also a limitation in this study. Some HR

professionals may have been hesitant to share practices that may not have position their

organizations favorably, even with assurances of confidentiality. Finally, as this study was

likely to participate.

                                     Chapter 2: Literature Review

       Multiple Sclerosis (MS) is the fastest-growing neurodegenerative disease among young

people (Ghasemi et al., 2017). Prior to the worldwide pandemic and the need for businesses to

enact work-from-home mandates, people with MS (PwMS) in traditional office environments

were forced to leave their positions due to physical and cognitive fatigue.

       Providing appropriate workplace accommodations may be a way to help individuals with

cognitive deficits remain in their professional positions longer (Bessing et al., 2022). This

literature review begins with an investigation of the experiences of PwMS in the workplace to

better understand why they are leaving their positions as critical background for this study. It

then explores what has been studied about psychological safety and empathy in the workplace.

Finally, this chapter outlines the role of HRD in supporting employees in the workplace and the

                     Empathy in the Workplace and Psychological Safety

       This first stream will discuss research in the topics of empathy in the workplace and

psychological safety. This stream will define the terms and attempt to describe what is

understood about these concepts. The stream will finally compare the two and attempt to

demonstrate how they are different but both integral in the workplace.

Empathy in the Workplace

       Empathy in the workplace is a driving factor in professional settings across the world

(Clark et al., 2018). Empathy is classified in two ways: cognitive and emotional. Cognitive

empathy is defined by Clark et al. (2018) as the ability to understand or the state of

                                                                                                     20


understanding others’ feelings. Emotional empathy occurs when one individual feels as though

another’s emotions are so strong they are almost contagious (Clark et al., 2018). Empathy in the

workplace is “a critical construct for predicting organizational behavior, including leader-

member exchange, crisis management, corporate philanthropy, and forgiveness” (Clark et al.,

2018, p. 166). Employees who feel supported demonstrate increased productivity and exceed

expectations across multiple measures (Clark et al., 2018).

       Empathy has also been found to prevent burnout in the workplace. Smits et al. (2022)

discussed the relationship between meaningful professional development and decreasing burnout

in teachers. In this study, the causes of burnout were high workloads, time pressure, and

emotional dysregulation. Smits et al. (2022) reported that when there is a lack of empathy in the

workplace, employees feel the need to hide their true emotions, which can lead to emotional

exhaustion and burnout. Smits et al. (2022) found that by increasing social interactions and

providing opportunities for faculty to share their expertise, there was an observed decrease in

reported feelings of burnout. Zavaglia (2023), who explained how negative emotions come with

a lack of trust in relationships in the workplace, noting that “when workers lack an open

environment to voice their opinions and be heard, they can experience emotions associated with

mistrust such as anxiety, oppression or fear” (p. 38)

       While studies have shown the importance of empathy in the workplace, it has been more

difficult to identify how to facilitate it. In his research, Holt (2022) highlighted the importance of

genuine personal relationships in the workplace as one way to foster empathy in a professional

setting. Holt (2022) also cited listening as the key skill set to utilize when building empathy in

the workplace. Without active listening, which involves the intent to understand, it will be

difficult to facilitate empathy between employees. Caffrey (2023) also researched empathy in the

                                                                                                     21


workplace through empathetic leadership, which he described as “involving the expression of

understanding on an emotional level combined with creating a psychologically safe space

through words and actions” (Caffrey, 2023). The researcher provided the example of an

employer speaking to an employee in a way that seeks understanding while also demonstrating

support from the organization. Caffrey (2023) also suggested providing empathy training for

leadership to support their growth in this style of communication. Similar to Caffrey’s (2023)

findings, Edmonson (2018) found that empathy in the workplace also supports increased

psychological safety.

Psychological Safety in the Workplace

         As defined previously, psychological safety is the employee’s believed ability to engage

in voicing behaviors such as speaking up, asking questions, departing from the status quo, or

even making mistakes without fear of punishment or mockery (Edmondson, 2018). While an

attempt to create a straight timeline in the study of psychological safety may prove difficult, it

can be traced back to Goffman’s (1955) work on team relationships in organizations. As

Edmondson’s research is the basis for understanding psychological safety today, we can look to

what she considers foundational research from Schein and Bennis (1965), which identified

psychological safety as essential in fostering security and motivation in people. Edmondson’s

(2018) research was conducted primarily in healthcare settings, where it was found that the best

teams were not making fewer mistakes than their less productive or less successful counterparts.

They were, however, reporting them more. These teams felt safe to report mistakes to learn from

them. From these reported mistakes, employees learned and grew as a unit, becoming more

productive, less wasteful, and providing better care for patients.

                                                                                                     22


        Psychological safety has been positively linked with high-quality communication, trust,

decision-making (O’Donovan & McAuliffe, 2020), and the overall effectiveness of a team

(Shuffler et al., 2018). By increasing communication and trust, teams are able to work more as a

cohesive unit rather than be concerned with the fear of humiliation or punishment based on

increased voicing behaviors.

        Also related to psychological safety is Lee’s (2021) claim that positive behaviors increase

positive emotions, resulting in increased psychological safety. Lee noted specific actions that are

viewed as psychologically safe in the workplace: expressing care, collective interest, and

supporting co-workers. It was found that employees felt similar emotions based on their

manager’s behaviors when applied consistently across the team (Lee, 2021). Shuffler et al.

(2018) identified that when employees feel able to question procedures or share mistakes, the

team can become more effective, reporting also the need to consider different cognitive styles

and mental abilities. One could argue that this classification fits individuals with cognitive

deficits and other invisible disabilities. It is not just within the team individuals need to feel

psychologically safe, but also from leadership.

Psychological Safety and Leadership

        Edmondson et al. (2023) have also examined how psychological safety has changed since

the COVID-19 pandemic, noting an enhanced need for team learning, unity, security, and

freedom. Her findings were in line with what Caffrey (2023) discussed previously about

empathetic communication and centered on the importance of feedback seeking and sharing from

leadership. It was determined that leaders normalized and modeled vulnerability and encouraged

it in their employees, when they openly discussed feedback, both positive and negative,

                                                                                                     23


leadership had received. Zavaglia (2023), also found that leadership was integral to building a

psychologically safe environment for the workplace, post COVID-19.

        Frazier et al. (2016) emphasized how employees need to be free from the fear of

retribution from leadership or coworkers in psychological safety. It is under these conditions that

an individual would be able to disclose their diagnosis and request appropriate accommodations.

Frazier and Tupper (2018) also identified how psychological safety mattered in terms of

“prosocial behaviors” from leadership, defined as helping behaviors, linking accommodations,

and promoting psychological safety in the workplace.

        Certain leadership styles are known to encourage psychological safety in the workplace

(Edmondson, 2018). Leaders in environments that are perceived as psychologically safe build

inclusivity into the overall strategy of their organizations (Randal et al., 2018). Inclusive leaders

foster their employees' sense of belonging, increasing their confidence in their abilities to take

risks at work (Randal et al., 2018). These leaders also support justice and equity as a part of the

employee experience (Randal et al., 2018). Inclusive leaders not only encourage diversity but

seek it out through recruiting and hiring practices to target individuals with invisible disabilities

(Bruyère, 2018). It is this style of leadership, as well as other human resources development

initiatives, that can encourage a more psychologically safe workplace for individuals with

invisible disabilities.

Stream Summary

        This stream defined and discussed the concepts of psychological safety and empathy,

noting that psychological safety relies on empathy and that empathy relies on the ability to think

of others and attempt to understand how they feel (Edmondson, 2018). Through empathy and,

subsequently, psychological safety in the workplace, employees trust each other and allow one

                                                                                                   24


another to show up as their true selves (Frazier et al., 2016). It is in a psychologically safe work

environment that employees feel they can stand out (Edmondson, 2018). As discussed in the

previous stream, PwMS feel as though they are calling attention to themselves when they

disclose their diagnoses.

       Organizations that have empathy and psychological safety in the workplace demonstrate

decreased reported burnout and high levels of productivity from employees (Clarke et al., 2018;

Smits et al., 2022). While leadership was identified as setting the stage for psychological safety

(Randal et al., 2018), who else is responsible for the overall implementation of strategies that

would affect the psychological safety and empathy levels in a workplace? The next stream will

investigate the role of the human resource development (HRD) professional in establishing the

culture of an organization and the overall experience of the employees. It will also address the

impact of DEI initiatives on PwMS.

      The Role of Human Resources Development in Supporting Employees and DEI

       Within the field of human resources, Jena et al. (2018) found a strong relationship

between employee engagement, employee trust, and organizational trust. Their research also

indicated that employees' psychological well-being is integral to building employee trust and

engagement. Jena et al. (2018) defined psychological well-being as feelings of safety and

belonging to one’s community, similar to psychological safety. Employers structure the

workplace environment and culture; they have control of the physical dangers (Balfour & Fuller,

2010), as well as the psycho-emotional ones.

       In Cooke et al.’s (2022) discussion of the importance of Human Centered Human

Resources Development (HCHRD), the authors report the aims of this style of HRD are “(a)

increasing investment in people’s capabilities; (b) increasing investment in the institutions of

                                                                                                  25


work; and (c) increasing investment in decent and sustainable work” (p. 7). HCHRD focuses on

building up employees as their best selves in order to produce the best product. HCHRD

practices emphasize the importance of supporting an employee as a whole person, not just as a

cog in the machine. Cooke et al. (2022) expressed that “a human-centered approach to HRM is

one that invests in people to equip them with employable skills and capabilities and that provides

them with decent and sustainable employment opportunities” (p. 9).

Link Between Leadership and HRD

       In 2014, Baek and Kim introduced the theory of stakeholder-based human resources

development (SBHRD), which emphasizes pushing forward the well-being of employees by

utilizing all stakeholders related to the business to increase accountability and ethical

considerations at all levels. In this type of system, all stakeholders are represented, and

accountability is enhanced through transparency of action. Frye (2020) continued this line of

thinking and argued the need for increased ethical consideration at the top of all business

hierarchies to allow for a trickle-down effect to take place and permeate into all levels of

decision-making.

       As noted in the previous stream, there is a distinct link between transparent leadership

and the psychological well-being of employees (Jena et al., 2018). When leadership is open and

honest with employees, there is a decrease in frustration and anxiety, which increases employee

attention and focus on an organization’s primary mission. Jena et al. (2018) found that

employees’ perceived well-being was just as important as physical safety to build employee and

organizational trust. As mentioned above, HRD focuses on employee engagement, employee

trust, and organizational trust, all of which depend upon psychological safety. Thus, the

                                                                                                      26


responsibility to build a psychologically safe environment may start with leadership but

inevitably falls on the HRD professional.

       Lien (2022) outlined how organizations can increase the psychological safety of all

employees, describing, as an example, encouraging interpersonal relationships through in-person

interactions such as team “health checks” to identify how individuals and the team as a whole are

functioning. She also encouraged leadership to be transparent about expectations and welcome

feedback from employees at all levels. Lien (2022) emphasized the importance of “listening with

curiosity” and “leading by example” (p. 13) to promote a psychologically safe workplace

       In a similar study, Jiang et al. (2018) surveyed randomized employees throughout the

United States and found that trust, authentic leadership, and transparent organizational

communication increased employee engagement. Yet again, the psychological well-being of

employees is found to be a critical factor in increasing employee engagement, leading to

increased productivity and a decrease in employee attrition. As in Jena et al.’s (2018) research,

Jiang et al. (2018) identified the control an employer has over employees' physical and

psychological well-being. Leadership sets the tone for attitudes in all activities in the business,

even at the training level but the HRD professional is responsible for building this culture

through effective initiatives and strategies.

HRD as DEI Initiatives for Individuals with Invisible Disabilities

       A significant part of HR’s role in an organization is the development and training of staff,

but what if the very training meant to inspire and educate was mired in mental models built on

discrimination and prejudice? Zurn et al. (2022) identified stereotypes and ablism as barriers for

individuals with disabilities, especially those with cognitive deficits. The researchers discovered

physical disabilities were more readily accommodated than cognitive deficits and mental health

                                                                                                     27


disorders. Hyensi et al. (2023) argued that including disability identities, specifically those with

cognitive deficits, in DEI initiatives provides a level of representation, education, and equitable

treatment to individuals with invisible disabilities.

       Individuals with invisible disabilities fear discrimination based on their cognitive deficits

as they view them as moral failings rather than symptom presentations (McIntosh et al., 2023).

However, efforts to recruit and hire individuals with invisible disabilities have increased in

recent years based on a better understanding of the strengths of this population (Vogus & Taylor,

2018). Individuals with invisible disabilities report increased psychological safety when

representation is “part of the organization’s definition of diversity, general diversity training”

(Vogus & Taylor, 2018, p. 516). It is important for organizations to continuously seek out the

voices of individuals with invisible disabilities and reflect on their experiences to effectively

support this population in the workplace (Vogus & Taylor, 2018). Similar to DEI initiatives

targeting gender, sexual orientation, and race, representation in these all-staff trainings acts as a

starting point for organizations.

       Though representation matters, Gupta and Priyadarshi (2020) found that there needs to be

more structural initiatives beyond representation and education. The authors found that people

with disabilities need organizations to now bridge the gap between understanding and action

(Gupta & Priyadarshi, 2020). Individuals interviewed by the authors reported a desire for

representation beyond training and suggested forums or informal groups of individuals with

disabilities to meet and discuss their current needs (Gupta & Priyadarshi, 2020).

       Overall, the responsibility to create and promote DEI lies with HRD professionals

(McDonald & Hite, 2018). Professionals in HRD fields may need to step outside their

organizations and work with healthcare professionals to better understand invisible disabilities

                                                                                                     28


(Beatty et al., 2019). Beatty et al. (2019) claim that through this type of collaboration, HRD

professionals would be able to better understand symptom presentation and how to better

anticipate the needs of individuals with disabilities. This reinforces the justification for this study

to further examine the needs of individuals with invisibilities. Gould et al. (2022) interviewed

individuals with disabilities to better understand how to support this population. They found that

increasing the heterogeneity of disability representation would benefit organizations.

Organizations would do well to increase the representation of different types of disabilities in

organization programming and education. People with cognitive deficits require different

supports than individuals with physical disabilities, so HRD professionals need to be educated on

how to work with and support a unique population of individuals with disabilities.

       The role of human resources development (HRD) personnel has always been tricky to

define. Are they there to serve the employees, or are they an instrument of the organization?

Cruikshank (1993) discussed the debate between entrepreneurs and social activists in the role of

adult education in the workplace; 20 years later, it still seems relevant. According to Cruikshank

(1993), entrepreneurs believe that the purpose of adult education is to increase the marketability

and usefulness of an employee and that by improving skills related to their business, they will

help increase profits. Alternatively, social activists believe education should be for the

betterment of oneself and ultimately serving society.

       Whatever side of the debate one lands on, the responsibility to determine organizational

culture is set by HRD (McDonald & Hite, 2018). It is with an awareness of this responsibility

that many HRD professionals are developing and implementing DEI initiatives that are slowly

beginning to include individuals with disabilities. However, that representation is homogeneous,

and individuals with unrepresented disabilities have started to ask for more (Gould et al., 2022).

                                                                                                     29


This demonstrates the importance of representation for disability identities in organizational DEI

initiatives. By including individuals with invisible disabilities, such as cognitive deficits in

recruiting, hiring, and training and development activities, employees can feel more represented

and safer to disclose (Bartram et al., 2021). Bartram et al. also reviewed individuals with ADHD

experiences in the workplace and found the education of all employees on invisible disabilities is

a signal to individuals with cognitive deficits that this is a safe environment to disclose.

       This stream introduced and described multiple HRD studies that hold the HRD

professional’s responsibilities involve supporting employee engagement and developing

employee and organizational trust. Theories such as HCHRD (Cooke et al., 2022) and SBHRD

       After establishing theories on overall HRD programming, the stream took a closer look

                            Experiences of PwMS in the Workplace

       This stream will document the current literature on PwMS's experience as they navigate

the culture of a professional environment. It will explore challenges they face through previous

research completed in this realm. People with invisible disabilities like MS face different kinds

of discrimination in the workplace (MS Society, n.d.). One type of discrimination comes from an

assumption made by coworkers that PwMS are unable to be fully productive – and that a lack of

productivity places an unfair workload on others (Lee et al., 2019). Lee et al. (2019) found that

these assumptions lead to microaggressions in the workplace. Microaggressions are “subtle

forms of discrimination and include verbal, nonverbal, or environmental slights that convey

disparaging messages to people based upon their group membership, such as disability” (Lee et

al., 2019, p. 1). Some of the more common microaggressions that study identified were social

distance, denial, assumption of disability status, lack of awareness, and made to feel like a

second class (Lee et al., 2019). PwMS are known to be concerned with how their colleagues

would have to increase their workload to compensate based on requested accommodations

(Kroger & Coetzee, 2021). Despite this concern with the optics of disclosure, research shows

individuals who receive accommodations demonstrate increased productivity and career

longevity (Dong & Mamboleo, 2023).

       Kroger and Coetzee (2021) uncovered feelings of guilt among PwMS when requesting

accommodations and a fear of appearing unable to complete their duties as assigned, not wanting

to be perceived as less than by their coworkers. PwMS reported overcompensating in the

workplace to prove to their coworkers that they were able to keep up both mentally and

physically (Kroger & Coetzee, 2021). Examples of overcompensation include staying late at

work, decreasing breaks, and taking on more projects than able to. This can be dangerous for

                                                                                                   31


PwMS as stress and overexertion can cause symptom exacerbation, leading to increased

symptom presentation (Rumrill, 2021). In Kroger and Coetzee’s (2021) study, a majority of

participants expressed concern for the future and not being able to maintain working at their

current level based on how their MS might progress (Kroger & Coetzee, 2021).

       In their research, Dong and Mamboleo (2023) interviewed PwMS, who were currently

working in a professional setting, to identify personality traits of the individuals who were more

likely to disclose their diagnoses. They found that individuals who had a more positive outlook

expectancy, higher levels of self-efficacy, and increased knowledge of their rights under the

Americans with Disability Act (ADA) were more likely to disclose their diagnoses. They also

identified the need to research further into the environments that allow PwMS to disclose their

disability, as the onus should not be solely on the individual but rather on the organization as

well (Dong & Mamboleo, 2023), an area this study intends to address.

       Systemic failures may also pose difficulties for PwMS when navigating accommodation

requests (Lee et al., 2019). An example of a systemic failure would be a lack of education or

awareness of diagnosis in leadership. As well, PwMS report challenges working with employers

who are uneducated in their particular diagnosis. Bonaccio et al. (2020) identified key concerns

employers have when working with individuals with disabilities, one of which is that they feel

unprepared to support their employees with disabilities based on a lack of training and education

in accommodations and/or employee diagnoses. The researchers also cited the number of

qualified candidates with disabilities, the cost of accommodations, and the level of qualification

of individuals with disabilities. Their research explained the need to better educate employers on

their employees’ needs, as these concerns centered around the employers’ lack of education and

training in working with individuals with disabilities.

                                                                                                     32


       In her work, Syma (2019) found there may be a fear of resentment or discrimination

when individuals with invisible disabilities request an accommodation. However, she outlined a

specific example in a case study when disclosing a disability and requesting an accommodation

may have saved an employee’s life. In this case study, the employee shared with her supervisor

her diagnosis and symptoms to be aware of, which allowed the supervisor to take the employee

to the hospital when she demonstrated increased severity of symptoms. While employees may

feel as though their colleagues would view their accommodations as “unfair,” Syma (2019) case

study demonstrates how educating leadership and increasing their awareness of their employees’

needs can be crucial in the workplace.

       PwMS may be unsure of how to initiate disclosure and requests for accommodations

based on a lack of education provided by the workplace (Syma, 2019; Lee et al., 2019; Kroger &

Coetzee, 2021). PwMS are generally aware, however, of how accommodations would serve them

in the workplace (Rumrill et al., 2021). Accommodations for cognitive deficits are the primary

accommodation requested by PwMS (Rumrill et al. (2021). Raggi et al. (2016) identified fatigue

as the primary factor of productivity loss. This is due to the difference in how PwMS experience

fatigue due to their symptom presentation (Roman et al., 2022). With a neurodegenerative

disease such as MS, many aspects of cognitive skills take more resources for PwMS to

participate in activities of daily life, resulting in higher and more frequent reports of fatigue

(Roman et al., 2022).

       As noted in Chapter 1, MS can present differently (Ghasemi et al., 2017, p.1), creating

the need for unique types of accommodations, particularly for those with cognitive deficits.

Workplace needs are also specific. Some individuals may need strategies for memory, some for

attention, and others for social skills. While there is an observable benefit for individuals with

                                                                                                  33


MS who stay at work (Conradsson et al., 2020; Jaworski et al., 2021), difficulty without

appropriate accommodations, accommodations alone may not be enough to aid PwMS in

remaining in their positions.

       It has also been reported that accommodations were not found to promote job satisfaction

or longevity in PwMS (Leslie et al., 20105). In their study of more than 700 PwMS, a majority

found workplace accommodations to be less successful than those who did not request such.

Leslie et al.’s findings suggest the need for additional research, as Syma (2019) found that

ignoring the needs of individuals with invisible diagnoses can cost employers money and create

toxic working environments for all employees.

       Dong and Mamboleo (2023) identified the key traits of PwMS who do disclose their

diagnoses and receive accommodations. The researchers discussed demographic factors like age

and gender but also looked at psychosocial traits. They found the traits that individuals

requesting accommodations in the workplace typically demonstrated self-efficacy, knowledge of

the ADA/accommodation process, job satisfaction, and positive/negative affect. They also

identified key environmental factors that aided individuals in their decision-making related to

accommodations, one of which was psychological safety. PwMS described how psychological

safety in the workplace made them feel as though they could disclose their diagnoses in order to

request accommodations. As well, Gill and Hynes (2021) cited the need for stronger workplace

relations and stricter boundaries for work/work-life balance, and, important to this study, the

need for appropriate accommodations in the workplace.

                               Chapter 3: Research Methodology
       The purpose of this study was to understand the relationship of a psychologically safe

work environment in which PwMS feel able to disclose their disability to their employers so that

they are able to maintain employment and independence. Specifically, this study sought to

understand the role of human resources departments in creating such environments. The results

of this study intended to inform the practices of human resources departments while supporting

the needs of individuals with MS to remain in the workforce. Individuals with “invisible

disabilities,” who present with cognitive deficits, such as MS, are leaving or decreasing their

employment status rather than disclosing their diagnoses due to fear, internalized ableism, and a

lack of education on disclosing and accommodations in the workplace (Syma, 2019). The

guiding research questions for this research were:

       RQ1 How do HR professionals define and operationalize a psychologically safe

workplace?

       RQ2 How do these practices integrate the representation of employees with cognitive

deficits?

       RQ3 How do HR professionals describe the inclusion of employees with cognitive

deficits in DEI initiatives?

       This chapter explains the research methodology for this study. The research design and

rationale will be reviewed, followed by descriptions of the research methods and data collection.

Finally, the limitations and ethical considerations will be discussed.

                                 Research Design and Rationale

       A phenomenological study seeks to understand a lived experience (Lin, 2013). Moran

(2002) defines phenomenology as “the attempt to get to the truth of matters, to describe

phenomena, in the broadest sense as whatever appears in the manner in which it appears, that is

                                                                                                       37


as it manifests itself to consciousness, to the experiencer” (p. 4). Through the understanding of

the shared experience of several participants, researchers are able to distill the essence beyond

what ordinary observations would provide (Lin, 2013). It is because of this that a

phenomenological study was chosen for the research.

          The phenomenon that was studied is the experience of HR professionals and their effort

to facilitate psychological safety in the workplace for individuals with invisible disabilities like

cognitive deficits. The researcher attempted to distill the essential truths of these experiences in

order to better understand if and where there was a workplace breakdown in creating

psychological safety for individuals with invisible disabilities. As explained in Chapter 1,

individuals with invisible disabilities like Multiple Sclerosis (MS) are decreasing their

employment or leaving their positions altogether, demonstrating the potential for unmet needs by

employees, and it was the researcher’s goal to understand why.

          By interviewing HR professionals across various disciplines and industries, the

researcher presented a better understanding of what efforts were being made to establish

psychological safety in the workplace for individuals with invisible disabilities. Research shows

that individuals with invisible disabilities are more comfortable disclosing a disability and, in

turn, able to request reasonable accommodations when they experience psychological safety

(Bonaccio et al., 2020). Disclosure and accommodations could lead to an individual being able to

remain in their position longer and increase their quality of life. It is the researcher’s intention to

utilize interviews with HR professionals whose lived experience will shed light on what is really

happening in the workplace that either promotes or acts as a barrier to increasing psychological

safety.

                                Population and Sample Description

                                                                                                 38


       The focus of this study was on the experience of Human Resource professionals whose

job responsibilities include climate and culture as they relate to the establishment of

psychological safety for individuals with invisible disabilities like cognitive deficits. The

population of this study was individuals involved with recruitment, hiring, onboarding, training

and development in some capacity. While they may not have been fully responsible for all these

tasks, they fell within the scope of their responsibilities. The researcher worked with 13

individuals to ascertain enough experiences to distill the phenomenon being studied into its truest

form while still capturing the larger themes described by the participants. The qualified

participants were

   •   Previously (within the past year) or currently employed in the HR field for at least 3

       years;

   •   Worked in a setting where employees work in person/on-site a majority of the week;

   •   Worked with a staff 10 or more of paid employees; and

   •   Worked at a site as the specific individual assigned as the Human Resources

       representative.

       The researcher used purposive sampling. Participants were identified based on their roles

as HR professionals at their organization as described above. The researcher also initially located

participants through personal and social networks and then utilized “snowball sampling” in order

to find more participants. Collaborating with participants to identify more potential participants

from their personal and professional networks allowed the researcher to connect with more

potential participants (Parker et al., 2019). Widening the network allowed the researcher to

include unexpected perspectives based on limited connection to this field.

Site Description and Access

       There was no specific site for this study. Potential participants were recruited through the

                                         Research Method

       This study involved a single research method, semi-structured interviews, which are

deemed to best identify the lived experiences of individuals. This qualitative, phenomenological

study focused primarily on capturing the essence of the knowledge and practice of HR

professionals who are responsible for fostering a psychologically safe workplace.

Semi-Structured Interviews

       Interviews were utilized to collect the experiences, expectations, and perceptions of the

13 participants. Interviews were at least 60 minutes in length and video recorded through Zoom

and then transcribed through Zoom’s automated transcription service, Otter-AI. The recordings

were then downloaded and saved to the researcher’s Drexel University password-protected

OneDrive account. This is a secure drive that only the researcher and her Supervising Professor

had access to in order to maintain the confidentiality of the participants.

Instrument Description

       The interview instrument, attached as Appendix A, consisted of 7 questions to gain

insight into how HR professionals foster psychological safety in the workplace for people with

cognitive deficits in relation to disclosing disabilities to request accommodations. Questions

focused on identifying initiatives and strategies implemented by the participants as HR

professionals in terms of creating psychological safety in the workplace. The participants were

also asked if and how they have worked with employees who disclosed disabilities and to

describe their organization’s process of accommodations in the workplace. The researcher also

attempted to understand if and how individuals with invisible disabilities like cognitive deficits

                                                                                                      40


are considered in DEI initiatives at the participants' organization. Questions were semi-structured

and allowed for a conversational discourse to educate the researcher so that she could begin to

see the essence of the phenomenon.

Participant Identification, Selection, and Invitation

        Participants were identified through the personal and social networks of the researcher.

The researcher provided the qualifications listed above to HRD professionals known to the

researcher both professionally and personally. After reviewing the qualifications listed above

with the researcher and if the HRD professionals report matching the qualifications listed above,

they were asked to participate in the interview. Once participants were identified, they were

invited through email, as found in Appendix B, to participate in semi-structured interviews

through Zoom. Participants were also sent a consent form found in Appendix C describing the

purpose and scope of the study, explaining their rights as participants in this study. Participants

were educated and asked to confirm their understanding of the requirements of the study such as

access to a device for video chatting, time requirements, and the potential for a follow up

interview. Participants who agreed were scheduled for their initial interview based on their

availability.

Data Collection

        All interviews took place at a date and time convenient to both the participant and the

researcher. Interviews were recorded through Zoom. The researcher also recorded the interview

with a secondary audio recording device as a backup to the Zoom recording and transcript. At

the start of each interview, the researcher received verbal consent from the participants to record

the interviews and use the information from the interviews in the study. The researcher also

reminded the participants of the agreed-upon length of interviews (at least 60 minutes) and the

                                                                                                     41


potential for any follow-up interviews based on the information discussed in the interviews. The

researcher used Drexel University’s Zoom which allowed for video and audio recording of the

interviews. The recordings were then downloaded and saved to the researcher’s Drexel

University password-protected OneDrive account. This is a secure drive that only the researcher

and her Supervising Professor have access to in order to maintain the confidentiality of the

participants.

                                      Researcher’s Journal

        The researcher took notes based on information presented in semi-structured interviews.

The goal was to participate fully in a conversational discourse format, but the researcher took

notes of patterns, interpretations, and other reflections as needed. The researcher also set aside

time after each interview, both immediately after and a day or two after, in order to reflect upon

the experience as well as create follow-up questions, identify patterns, or expand on concepts

discussed in previous interviews. This form of bracketing (Lin, 2013) allowed the researcher to

remain open to different perspectives and be aware of any biases formulating throughout the

interview process. The researcher’s journal also served to identify any emergent themes in the

data. This helped the researcher when it was time to code and analyze the data during the

inductive coding of the transcriptions. All hard copies of notes and journaling from the

researcher were stored in a locked area until they were able to be scanned and stored with the

electronic data on Drexel’s OneDrive. All electronic data is stored on Drexel’s OneDrive, which

is password-protected and encrypted. Only the researcher and Supervising Professor have access

to this data.

                                           Data Analysis

                                                                                                   42


        The researcher utilized transcription services from Zoom recordings. The researcher

reviewed the transcriptions and compared them to the video recordings to ensure accuracy. The

researcher also transferred any hard copies of the researcher’s journal into an electronic file. All

recordings, transcriptions, and researcher’s journal were uploaded to Drexel’s OneDrive, a

password-protected and encrypted drive to which only the researcher and Supervising Professor

have access.

        The researcher used inductive coding in the first rounds of coding in order to review the

data without applying any bias or preconceived notions to the information provided by the

participants. The researcher began with In Vivo coding. This style of coding places emphasis on

the specific words spoken by the participants (Saldaña, 2021). It is often used to understand

stories and concepts through the actual words used by the population to describe the specific

phenomenon being studied.

        The researcher then utilized Values coding. This helped the researcher identify how the

participants valued working with and supporting individuals with invisible disabilities. People

are often driven by their values, and values coding illuminates any unconscious bias participants

have related to working with individuals with invisible disabilities (Saldaña, 2021).

Subsequently, this bias was viewed as potentially a limiting factor for these professionals that

could help describe why a professional environment is not psychologically safe for individuals

with invisible disabilities.

        The researcher then utilized NVivo to organize and apply auto-coding options, which

helped to identify patterns in the experiences, expectations, and perceptions shared by each

participant through the interviews and the researcher’s journal. The data was then preliminarily

compiled into identifiable patterns based on shared experiences.

                                                                                                    43


       Finally, the researcher applied deductive coding. This allowed her to apply research

questions and patterns previously identified to the data, resulting in a combination of deductive

and pattern coding. While completing multiple rounds of coding, the researcher reviewed notes

from her journal in order to make connections between the patterns and themes initially

identified during the interviews and the ones identified through coding.

                                     Methodological Limitations

       As is typical in a phenomenological study, a limitation is the sample size. To study a

phenomenon, one needs a large sample size of experiences to accurately describe the

phenomenon. The small sample size means that the results may not be transferable, and the

results will not be generalizable.

       Another limitation was that participants were self-selected and voluntary and limited to

the researcher’s personal and social network. To decrease the effects of this limitation, the

researcher utilized snowball sampling, using participants and their network of fellow HR

professionals to open the participant pool to more than just individuals available to the

researcher.

                                       Ethical Considerations

       The researcher completed the CITI training and the IRB process as noted in the timeline

after the proposal was approved. In considering the research design for this study, ethical

standards such as the Belmont Study were highly scrutinized. The researcher maintained respect

for persons, beneficence, and justice throughout the study. Maintaining the confidentiality of the

participants was the researcher’s primary concern. Pseudonyms were used to refer to all

participants in the final report. The researcher held the identity of each participant’s organization

confidential by eliminating any identifying contextual description and, where applicable, the

                                                                                                    44


application of pseudonyms. All recordings, transcriptions, and files with participants' information

were stored on Drexel’s OneDrive, a password-protected and encrypted electronic storage

system to which only the researcher and Supervising Professor have access. This information

will be maintained for three years prior to destruction.

       A phenomenological study honors the lived experiences of people as a true data point.

This phenomenological study sought to understand why people with cognitive deficits are not

Facilitating Psychological Safety in the Workplace
       Participants provided descriptions of psychologically safe environments in their work

environments, often describing the need to create an open and understanding environment to

better serve employees within the scope of HR’s ability. A majority of the participants described

building a culture of safety by making everyone feel seen and heard while also building trust.

Participants also described personal connections to disabilities in the workplace, their own

personal journey, or that of a loved one. Finally, participants shared professional expectations of

empathy and how their organizations structured policies to serve employees with empathy as a

guiding force.

Defining Psychological Safety with Trust
       When asked to describe their idea of a psychologically safe environment, all thirteen

participants were able to define psychological safety. Many spoke of the ability for all employees

                                                                                                      48


to feel comfortable bringing their whole selves to work. Participants cited the ability for

employees to present as their physical selves: wearing their hair a certain way, feeling safe as a

person of color, or having a physical disability and being able to access specific areas of their

workspace. Most participants also identified aspects such as an employee’s perceived ability to

fail or request help in the workplace.

       Psychological safety was defined by Participant A as “being able to try something, fail at

it, get the feedback and improve from there, and not be afraid that every mistake will be your last

mistake.” Participant H spoke of the need for trust in the organization: “… it really all starts with

trust and trust within the organization.” Other participants shared a similar view. They spoke of

the need for an organization to instill trust in their employees for them to be able to share their

needs as well as speak up when it mattered. Participant H described a companywide policy she

referred to as “Stop Job Authority:” that any employee has the right to stop work on any project

at any time as long as they believe it is the safe response. She described, “They can stop the job;

doesn't matter what your level is. It doesn't matter, the lowest level person out there working.

You have to, as a company, create that comfort and trust that I can stop a job.” In this example,

the participant described how their culture attempted to make employees feel safe to speak up.

       The participants explained that psychological safety was necessary for employees to

share concerns not just about physical safety, as Participant H described, but mental and

emotional well-being. Participant I described how employees need to feel safe to share “if

anything is going on, or they need any type of mental health help or anything like that.”

Participants identified this type of employee-sharing as how an organization learns from

mistakes.

       Participant K acknowledged caring and listening as important aspects an organization

brings to building a psychologically safe environment, describing psychological safety as when

someone “bring[s] your whole self, understanding that you may not always get to agreement, but

Professional Empathy

       Participants explained the ways that empathy appeared in the workplace in ways that

oriented to professional practices - as opposed to personal relationships. The concept of

professional empathy relates to how participants described organizational polices and procedures

that guide organizational behaviors and practices, that indirectly supported empathy in the

workplace. This section will describe policies and procedures developed by the organization that

lead to more empathetic organizational culture.

       ADA and Interactive Process. Participants described systems in place, such as policies

and procedures that allowed them to provide professional empathy in the workplace. They noted

that professional empathy does not require a personal relationship or understanding, but rather, it

guides HR professionals and leaders in providing an empathetic experience to employees.

       Participants all shared an understanding of the basic processes involved with disclosing

disabilities to secure accommodations in the workplace. A majority of participants reported that

employees need to initiate the process by disclosing their disability and that it is the

organization’s role to maintain the confidentiality of this disclosure throughout the process.

Participants expressed that disclosure was just the beginning of the process. Though some

organizations did not require a letter stating which accommodations the employee would need, a

majority of the participants interviewed stated a letter with a diagnosis was required to initiate

the process. Participant A shared an experience when she had to educate the employee on her

rights and the next steps in the process,

       It is totally legitimate and well within the company's rights to request a letter of

       medical necessity from the doctor. It doesn't have to disclose too much

       information; [it] simply has to confirm that there is a diagnosed disability. The

                                                                                                   56


       ideal scenario would be that it includes what the disability is, for example,

       diagnosed with Multiple Sclerosis, and currently, you know, engaged in treatment

       for this. And then the doctor's note can also request specific accommodation.

       They don't have to detail what that accommodation is, but the [doctor] can say

       something along the lines of ‘the employee requests [specific] accommodation to

       fulfill her job obligations,’ and those accommodations can be defined between the

       employee and the organization.

Some participants noted that the process can start even before the employee begins working at

the organization.

       When applying for work at Participant B’s organization, applicants can self-identify as

“disabled.” He described, “It's part of our applicant flow for new employees. So, if you're

coming and applying for a role, you can self-select a disability. It's private. Nobody would see

this other than you. It's more of a data point.” Though it may be a “data point,” as Participant B

puts it, this was described as the start of an individual’s accommodation process and is an

integral step in compliance with ADA legislation. The process is initiated once the disclosure is

made; this is when the process becomes different for each organization.

       Some organizations have informal meetings with employees once the initial disclosure is

made. In Participant D’s organization, this meeting is initiated by HR in order to coordinate the

appropriate

       They will do a Teams Meet message, or they'll be like, ‘Hey, can we talk real

       quick’ and we'll have a quick meeting and I provide them with all the facts of who

       to contact, and they go ahead and they reach out. So, the accommodation

                                                                                                57


       paperwork does not come through me personally, but I provide them with the

       information.

Participant F described using a third-party vendor to manage accommodations.

       The employee typically engages with their business partner, their people business

       partner … somebody in my role. And then we pull in our benefits team at that

       point. We have a third-party vendor that administers actually all of our leave of

       absences … as well as our accommodations. They submit electronically and talk

       through with the company what the issue is and who their physician is, and then

       the paperwork is sent out to the physician and collected back.

Once the paperwork is collected, most participants cited an “interactive process” for identifying

what accommodations will be made for the employee.

       Participants used the term “dialogue” to describe this portion of the process as they

benefitted from understanding what the employee needed to determine what the organization

could provide the employee. All participants explained that “reasonable accommodations” meant

that the accommodations must not put an “undue hardship” on the employer. However, they also

acknowledged the vagueness of these terms and how they are completely subjective to the

organization. Because of the ambiguous defining of terms, participants emphasized the

importance of the dialogue with the employee. Participant H described an example of this

interactive process she had been through.

       We did go through a process with him to determine if there were roles that could

       accommodate him with the disability that he had acquired from this accident. So

       he ended up choosing to remain out on total disability versus coming back to a job

       with any kind of accommodation.

In trying to find solutions to allow the employee to remain in his role, the interactive process

uncovered that not returning to work would be in the best interest of the employee. Participant H

continued to say, “You're supposed to offer that. I would also hope that that's just the formality,

and the real value in it is the discussion about what can we do to make you successful.” She

recognized the importance of the interactive process beyond legal compliance and more for the

understanding the discussion can bring. Participant L distilled the experience down to, “You

have to start with, ‘What are they asking for? What are their needs?’ and then go from there.”

Participant E described a compromise to find mutually beneficial solutions.

       That’s what we call the interactive process. It's very much a conversation. We're

       trying to find the right solution that they're getting what they need, while still

       being able to run our business. We'll find that happy medium.

These examples are specifically related to acquiring a workplace accommodation due to medical

necessity. By utilizing the ADA interactive process, organizations present a consistent way to

demonstrate empathy for their employees.

Participant B explained, “I think it's become a bit more of a big thing of, everybody's different.

Personal Empathy

       Participants explained the ways that empathy appeared in the workplace in ways that

oriented to personal beliefs - as opposed to professional practices. This concept of personal

empathy is one way the researcher organized how participants described how their own moral

and ethics guided them, separate from what the organization provided in the form of policies and

procedures.

                                                                                                     62


       Morals and Ethics. Participants also shared their ability to demonstrate empathy beyond

company policies and procedures. In their interviews, some participants responded they needed

to “do the right thing” for employees despite what the company’s policy stated. One participant

shared, “I think people just need to do the right thing.” She continued, “and we definitely want to

do what's right” when describing the accommodations process at her organization. Doing “the

right thing” was not something completely beholden to HR, however. Participant L described the

relationship between leadership and HR. “We can come together and say, what is the right thing

to do? Have we done it?” This idea of “the right thing” could be a compromise between what the

company needs and what the employee needs as well. As Participant I explained, “But we have

to have the team that voices their opinion, and says, this is the way we need to do this, this is

right.” The organization must define “the right thing” and employees also have to participate in

defining what it is as well. In defining “the right thing,”s HR professionals described using their

own personal experience as guidance.

Connection to Leadership

       While describing their role in facilitating a psychologically safe work environment,

participants also explained the importance of leadership in creating these environments.

Participants shared examples of leadership in their organization that they said exhibited empathy.

“middle management,” in how to be more empathetic and supportive of employees as they

navigate policies and programs provided by the organization. Finally, participants were able to

identify the level of influence Human Resources as a department had in the structure,

organization, and strategy of an organization; many noted that HR “has a seat at the table.”

Changing Role of HR

       Many participants were unable to describe HR in a consistent manner. Some cited HR’s

need to support the organization coming into conflict with their desire and responsibility to

provide guidance for the employees. Some participants reported the need to “do the right thing”

but struggled with identifying who they needed to “do the right thing” for: the employee or the

employer. Participants also shared HR’s role as creative problem solvers when they felt stuck

between serving the needs of the business while also supporting employees. Finally, participants

described the human aspect of HR, sharing their concerns with burnout, their own perceptions of

the HR role, and the skills involved with navigating the changing role of HR.

       Demonstrating their commitment to their employees and employers, the participants

how HR identified a solution that allowed the employee to remain productive while also

injustices played a part in changing their roles. Participant B shared the recent change in his

organization from accommodations to accessibility.,

         4 or 5 years ago, they got their hands around it. Now there's an entire accessibility

         HR function, a team that manages on-site accommodations that go into all the

         facilities and what HR leaders can actually use for accommodating people.

Participant D shared a similar mindset shift when she replaced the previous HR professional. She

explained that her organization wanted to change HR’s focus, wanting “someone who’s more

people-oriented.” She had been able to personalize working schedules, allowing for more

employees to work from home and also adjusting workplace environments based on the specific

                                                                                                   75


needs of employees. These creative solutions went beyond the “old school” view of HR.

Participant M described the transition for HR throughout her career.

        Years and years ago, earlier in my career, when a colleague had a heavy accent,

       the emphasis was on the colleague to figure out how to communicate better. That

       has had a 180 turn that everybody I interact with understands. It's their job to

       listen harder and try harder to hear right? That it's not on the colleague to figure

       out how to lose their accent, but it's on me to figure out how to hear through their

       accent.

She recognized in her interview that it was HR’s role to facilitate that change in mindset and help

find those creative solutions in the workplace. Participant D shared her own personal experience

with disclosing her disability,

       When they say, ‘Do you have a disability?’ I would say ‘Yes I do.’ It's who I am.

       It's fine, but I think that's more common in our generation and younger

       generations to be more open about that. But older generations aren't, and I always

       remember my mother tell me, don't put that on there. They're going to hold it

       against you.

       Due to the evolving role of HR, participants were affected by their own contradictory

feelings of trying to support their employees while also supporting the organization. Most

identified that the needs of the employee were often at odds with the needs of the organization.

Though they also cited this as a motivating factor to act as a creative problem solver for their

organization.

                                            Summary

                                                                                                   76


       This chapter identified the views shared by participants from their interviews. The

participants provided answers to better understand the role they took in facilitating psychological

safety in the workplace, with a focus on individuals with invisible disabilities. They shared that

psychological safety is not something that is only targeted for one specific group but rather

something that is built for everyone. The goal is to create relationships with individuals to

facilitate a psychologically safe environment, making everyone feel seen and welcomed.

       The individuals interviewed defined what they believed a psychologically safe

environment looked like, citing empathy or components of empathy such as trust. They also

shared that empathy is a key component to building a psychologically safe environment. It

requires a professional level of empathy involving specific policies and procedures that cue

employees into what is allowed and what is not. They explained that a level of personal empathy

needs to be present to facilitate a psychologically safe environment.

       Participants cited the importance of leadership in facilitating a psychologically safe

environment. They defined leadership's positive qualities and the role they play in an

organization’s culture. They explained that HR’s role is to coach leadership when they are

struggling to meet the organization’s expectation of psychological safety. This also led

participants to define the need for HR to be in consensus with leadership, often described as HR

having a “seat at the table” as a trusted professional in their organization.

       Finally, the participants were able to describe their own personal beliefs surrounding the

role of HR. Some discussed the difficulty that comes from external perceptions of their role.

Others shared differing levels of skepticism they feel is required for their role. Participants were

also able to identify lack of experience or exposure to individuals with invisible disabilities

makes working with them more difficult.

           Chapter 5: Results, Interpretations, Conclusions, and Recommendations

        This study sought to understand how HR professionals facilitate a psychologically safe

professional environment in order for individuals with cognitive deficits to disclose their

diagnoses to receive accommodations in the workplace. 13 HR professionals from different

organizations based in various sectors completed semi-structured one-to-one interviews via

Zoom in order to distill their essential experience in working with individuals with invisible

disabilities like cognitive deficits.

        The following questions guided this study:

            •   How do human resource professionals define and operationalize a psychologically

                safe workplace?

            •   How do human resource professionals integrate the representation of employees

                with cognitive deficits in psychologically safe practices?

            •   How do human resource professionals describe the inclusion of employees with

                cognitive deficits in DEI initiatives?

        This chapter will explore five key results identified in the study. The first result will

review the idea of a universal approach to creating psychological safety in the workplace. The

second result will expand on this finding by describing how this universal approach utilizes

empathy and trust as the foundations for a psychologically safe environment. The third result

will describe the evolving role of HR, the negative perceptions identified, and how this may be

detrimental to building trust between employees and the organization in the workplace. The

fourth result will continue with HR professionals’ perception of their roles by describing how

HR professionals tend to take a positional view on leadership in their organizations. The fifth

result focuses on the personal connection the participants reported to have with disabilities and

                                                                                                   79


how this may aid in building trust and an overall positive experience in working with individuals

with invisible disabilities. This chapter will then present the study’s conclusions as well as

recommendations for practice and future research.

Result #1: HR utilizes a universal approach to psychological safety in the hopes of

welcoming diversity and fostering an inclusive environment

       The participants shared that psychological safety was not targeted in specific groups in

the workplace. Rather, they believed psychological safety requires a universal approach for all

employees to feel welcomed. It is notable that the literature primarily refers to psychological

safety as “a group variable that describes team context” (Kim et al., 2020, p. 2). When studied,

psychological safety is often focused on team attitudes and beliefs (Edmondson, 2018; Shuffler

et al., 2018; Kim et al., 2020; O’Donovan & McAuliffe, 2020). It was found that employees felt

similar emotions based on their manager’s behaviors when applied consistently across the team

(Lee, 2021), similar to the findings in the participant interviews. Participants described their

strategies to build psychological safety for all employees rather than targeting specific groups or

individuals, demonstrating consistent behaviors across the team.

       What a person feels can be a highly individualized, personal experience (Baumeister et

al., 2007). However, Participant F approached it “through an agnostic lens of it doesn't matter

what you have. We wanted to treat people consistently and equally.”

       Also related to psychological safety is Lee’s (2021) claim that positive behaviors increase

positive emotions, resulting in increased psychological safety. Lee noted specific actions that are

viewed as psychologically safe in the workplace: expressing care, collective interest, and

supporting co-workers. Participant F described how she encourages managers she is coaching to

ask employees how they “like to work.” Do they like meeting face to face, talking on the phone,

                                                                                                  80


or communicating in written communication such as email or instant messaging text systems?

Participant F emphasized how she coaches the managers in her organization to ask this of all

their employees.

       Psychological safety is linked to the sense of belonging in an organization (Edmondson,

2018). Belonging is listed in Maslow’s Hierarchy of Needs (Maslow, 1943), believed to be

applicable to a majority of all people at some level (Dar & Sakthivel, 2022). Lee (2021), too,

supports the universal application of psychological safety in the workplace. This adds argument

to this study’s finding that psychological safety should be applied universally. As Participant K

stated, “We recognize that there are barriers for people. It's a perception whether you have

cognitive difficulties, whether a Black person or you’re female, we all have things that people

use from bias.” Participant M summed up this idea by stating, “The enterprise expectation is that

we're creating psychological safety for everyone.”

Result# 2: Empathy and trust are the foundation for building a universal approach to

psychologically safe environments in the workplace

       The participants shared what this researcher refers to as “professional” empathy, the

policies and procedures in place that support an employee as an individual in the workplace. This

is distinct from “personal” empathy, which are morals/ethics and personal beliefs and actions,

also referenced by participants.

       This study found that both “professional” and “personal” empathies are necessary for HR

professionals to do their jobs. They also cited a trusting relationship between employees and the

HR professional as foundational to creating psychological safety in the workplace. Participant C

shared while taking the perspective of an employee, “If I [the employee] don't have a trusting

relationship with you [HR professional], then I'm going to be mad. I'm going to be like, ‘Hey,

                                                                                                     81


man, why don't you like me?’ It can feel personal. It can feel painful.” Participant C illustrated

findings from Zavaglia (2023), who explained how negative emotions come with a lack of trust

in relationships in the workplace, noting that “when workers lack an open environment to voice

their opinions and be heard, they can experience emotions associated with mistrust such as

anxiety, oppression or fear” (p. 38). Conversely, a trusting relationship will increase positive

emotions such as openness and courage to voice behaviors such as questioning, standing out, and

identifying mistakes (Edmondson, 2018). Also, Smits et al. (2022) reported that when there is a

lack of empathy in the workplace, employees feel the need to hide their true emotions, which can

lead to emotional exhaustion and burnout.

       In this study, participants shared how they implemented empathy in the workplace by

engaging in an “interactive process” when working with individuals seeking workplace

accommodations. The participants described how they used active listening and various

communication strategies to find the best solution for both the employee and the organization.

Active communication has been found to increase psychological safety in the workplace

(Caffrey, 2023). Though some participants reported difficulty identifying and coaching empathy

in the workplace, Caffrey (2023) noted the potential of empathy training for those in leadership

roles to create a more empathetic climate and culture in an organization.

Result #3: A negative perception of a human resources department may impact its

effectiveness for employees with invisible disabilities

       Participants reported feeling as though employees' negative perception of HR challenged

their goal of building trusting relationships in the workplace. They acknowledged the difficulty

in balancing their roles in HR, feeling torn between supporting employees and upholding policies

                                                                                                  82


and procedures for their organizations. This presents an important backdrop to the disclosure of

invisible disabilities, particularly as it relates to building trust with employees.

        Holt (2022) highlighted the importance of genuine personal relationships in the

workplace as one way to foster empathy in a professional setting. Based on Holt’s (2022) work,

without trust, there may be no genuine, personal relationship between the HR professional and

the employee. Employees who distrust HR professionals based on negative perceptions will not

feel psychologically safe. Based on Dong and Mamboleo’s (2023) work, these individuals would

feel unable to disclose their disabilities to receive accommodations. The participants in this

research shared similar concerns from employees in their organizations that disclosure could be

used “as ammo” against them. Participant B consistently returned to this narrative during his

interview. He acknowledged that employees were aware of an unofficial culture that any sign of

“weakness” could be a reason not to promote someone in his organization. Psychologically safe

environments are marked by high-quality communication, trust, and decision-making

(Edmonson, 2018; O’Donovan & McAuliffe, 2020; Shuffler et al., 2018). Based on this

definition, one could conclude Participant B’s organization was not psychologically safe.

Result#4: HR professionals view leadership as positional and identify a marked difference

between their roles and decision-making leadership in their organization.

        The HR professionals in this study discussed the need for consistent, firm support from

leadership as a critical component of their ability to build trusting relationships with employees.

As discussed in Chapter 4, most participants shared the importance of HR’s role in key

organizational decisions, described as having “a seat at the table.” Several participants reported

feeling “torn” between supporting the organization and supporting employees. These were often

the participants who also shared disagreements in climate and culture between leadership and

                                                                                                     83


HR and who noted that their organizational culture was not likely to be described by employees

as psychologically safe.

       One key factor of psychological safety these participants noted was missing related to

empathy, explaining that empathy was not modeled by the leadership. As noted in Result #1,

empathy is a tool HR professionals use to demonstrate a welcoming environment to promote

inclusivity. As discussed in Chapter 2, leaders in environments that are perceived as

psychologically safe build inclusivity into the overall strategy of their organizations (Randal et

al., 2018). This is why HR professionals who work in organizations where leadership is not

aligned with psychological safety are unable to facilitate psychological safety despite their best

efforts. Ultimately, the HR professionals who felt psychological safety was lacking in their

organizations equated this to upper-echelon leaders who did not prioritize empathy as a tool to

demonstrate inclusivity in their organizations.

       The participants viewed leadership as positional. They believed that the only way to be a

leader is through authority, as shown in the examples provided and reflected in Frye’s (2020)

argument for increased ethical consideration at the top of all business hierarchies to allow for a

trickle-down effect to take place and permeate all levels of decision-making. It is not necessarily

authority figures who fall into this classification; any individual making decisions can affect the

psychological safety of an organization. If “leadership” matters to psychological safety

(Zavaglia, 2023) and HR does not feel like they are leaders, then it makes sense they do not feel

empowered to create psychological safety.

       But the literature shows exercising leadership is not dependent upon position (Caffrey,

2023; Edmondson, 2018; Randall et al., 2018,). One way an individual can be a leader in an

organization is by coaching and mentoring other individuals in the organization to encourage

                                                                                                   84


positive behaviors that promote psychological safety (Caffrey, 2023), similar to the coaching and

mentoring described by the participants in Chapter 4. Individuals can also act as creative

problem solvers and offer solutions to unique situations (Edmondson, 2018), again like the

actions described by the participants in Chapter 4.

       The participants were quick to point out the need for positive leadership to create a

psychologically safe environment in the workplace, and the literature confirms this need

(Edmondson, 2018; Caffrey, 2023; Zavaglia, 2023). The participants were also able to describe

their proximity to leadership and the need for leadership to be aligned with HR in order to

facilitate a psychologically safe environment in the workplace. However, the participants did not

demonstrate an understanding of their role as leaders. Through their described actions in Chapter

4, no matter their position or level of authority these participants indirectly defined themselves as

leaders but failed to make the connection.

Result #5: Personal Experience May Increase HR Professional’s Level of Empathy for

Individuals with Invisible Disabilities

        In their interviews, seven of the participants disclosed a personal connection to a

disability – a relative or friend or their own disability. These connections helped give them

perspective when working with individuals with invisible disabilities.

       The participants who shared a personal connection to a disability also claimed they were

able to identify cognitive deficits as “value add” to an individual. Participant K disclosed the

diagnoses of her children during her interview. She also shared how she believed individuals she

had hired with “cognitive deficits” were “rare birds” and added unique talents to the team based

on what others would perceive as negative traits. Participant D shared a similar perspective, after

disclosing her personal diagnosis she also shared how individuals with invisible disabilities, like

                                                                                                   85


Autism Spectrum Disorder, are actually recruited in her field based on the unique ways they can

identify patterns and analyze data.

       These participants also shared an awareness of the potential for employees to have

disabilities and anticipating their needs for accommodations. One participant recalled his surprise

at a previous organization when an employee in a wheelchair could not enter the building due to

physical barriers. In his current organization, he plans out his route with the physical capabilities

of the employee in mind when he gives tours to new hires. The participants shared that the

personal proximity to a disability helped to increase the awareness of disabilities overall in their

organizations. One participant shared he changed the route he took during a site tour based on an

experience his friend who was disabled had in the workplace. These participants described an

empathic approach to their HR responsibilities that was not noted in the participants who did not

share a personal connection to disability.

       Based on the findings, this study may conclude that experience makes for a deeper level

of empathy. While all participants shared “an understanding” of the “fear of discrimination”

employees with invisible disabilities may have in the workplace, it was the participants who

shared a personal connection to disability that were able to demonstrate trust in the employees,

anticipate employees’ needs, and support the employees in the examples they gave during their

interviews. They spoke of actively recruiting individuals with cognitive deficits, preparing for

disabilities that had not been disclosed, and offering actionable steps employees could use to

decrease frustration and anxiety in the workplace when accommodations were not an option.

       The participants who shared no personal experience with a disability also demonstrated

the most level of discomfort and difficulty identifying examples of working with an individual

with cognitive deficits in their workplace. Two different types of empathy were explained in

                                                                                                    86


Chapter 2: cognitive and emotional. Cognitive empathy is the ability to understand, or the state

of understanding others’ feelings. Emotional empathy occurs when one individual feels as

though another’s emotions are so strong they are almost contagious (Clark et al., 2018). In this

study, the most common empathy discussed was cognitive empathy. Participants shared that they

understood how employees felt, logically speaking. A majority of the participants reported

“building relationships” to demonstrate empathy in order to serve the employees of their

organization. Participant D described her role was “making sure that they have a comfort level so

that they have any questions they come to me”

Results Summary

       The results in this study focused on six concepts. The first result introduced the idea of a

universal approach utilized by the participants in the study to facilitate a psychologically safe

environment in the workplace. The second result described the universal approach’s utilization

of empathy and trust as a signal to employees that the workplace is a welcoming and inclusive

environment. The third result discussed the difficulty in building empathetic and trusting

relationships between HR and employees due to the negative perceptions surrounding the HR

professional’s role. The fourth result described a second barrier for HR professionals, their

inability to truly see themselves as leaders in their organization based on a traditional, positional

view on leadership. The fifth result identified personal experiences with individuals with

disabilities may increase empathy in HR professionals. In the conclusions section, these results

will be utilized to answer the guiding research questions.

                                            Conclusions

       This study described the experiences of 13 HR professionals and psychological safety for

individuals with cognitive deficits in the workplace. The participants defined psychological

                                                                                                      87


safety for all employees. They shared the importance of building psychological safety for

everyone and not focusing on one group in their efforts. Finally, they discussed their efforts in

the realm of DEI, which typically references race, ethnicity, and gender. While this study

attempted to understand how individuals with cognitive deficits were included in DEI initiatives,

this demographic was not referenced. This section presents conclusions as they relate to this

study’s guiding research questions.

RQ1: How do human resource professionals define and operationalize a psychologically

safe workplace?

        All participants reported familiarity with the concept of psychological safety without

clarification from the researcher. Most participants identified their organization as having a

psychologically safe environment, relating concepts such as empathy, trust, and open

communication to its presence. Participants shared the importance of an employee’s ability to

feel “seen” and their ability to bring their whole selves to the workplace. The participants

referred to caring and listening as well in their definitions. They also identified the ability to fail

and “not be afraid that every mistake will be your last mistake” and explained how a lack of fear

stems from trusting the organization. Based on the findings presented in Chapter 4, this study can

conclude that psychological safety hinges on trust.

        Empath and trust as concepts are easily defined but more difficult to describe through

specific actions in the workplace. In Chapter 4, the term “professional empathy” was used to

describe the policies and procedures in participants’ organizations. With respect to disclosing a

disability, the participants explained an “interactive process” of identifying the accommodation

needed and finding compromises based on open conversations with the requesting employee.

The participants also shared the use of resource/support groups created by employees but then

                                                                                                       88


supported by the organization. Finally, the participants described treating everyone “equally” as

a professional empathy tool.

       The professionals in this study appear to be moving toward a universal approach to

psychological safety. Thus, it is challenging to identify actions specific to individuals with

cognitive disabilities if empathy and trust are built uniquely for each employee. This study also

found that an overall negative perception of the HR professional role makes HR-based

psychological safety initiatives problematic. If employees have a negative perception of HR from

the start of their employment, the HR professional will not be able to build a trusting

relationship, and the employee may not feel the environment is psychologically safe.

       This study also uncovered the difficulty of operationalizing psychological safety practices

when HR professionals do not feel empowered in their roles as leaders. Steeped in traditional

views of positional leadership, the participants in this study did not feel able to try new practices

that deviate from the norm of what was expected at their organization. In contexts such as these,

positional leadership needs to have a strong presence and belief in the importance of

psychological safety in order to for to be felt in the workplace. It should also be noted, however,

that participants indirectly described themselves as leaders based on their ability to make

decisions and affect organizational behaviors. This disconnect between the view of leadership

and their ability to feel like leaders may affect how effective they feel they can be in their roles

to coach and support middle management.

RQ2: How do human resource professionals integrate the representation of employees with

cognitive deficits in psychologically safe practices?

       The participants in this study did not differentiate between employees with cognitive

deficits when considering psychologically safe practices in their organizations, with other

                                                                                                     89


employees. Rather, they focused on creating an environment that was psychologically safe for

all employees. They explained the need to establish a trusting and empathetic relationship

between employees and HR professionals so that no matter what the employee needed to share

or disclose, they would feel safe being able to be vulnerable with the organization.

        This lack of representation may also be related to a lack of awareness of the needs of

employees with cognitive deficits. The participants described how personal connections to

individuals with disabilities changed their behaviors when working with employees with

disabilities. However, this result of this study suggests that HR professionals who do not have

experience with individuals with cognitive deficits may not be as aware of this population,

making it difficult to anticipate their needs. This would make integrating the needs of employees

with cognitive deficits particularly difficult in psychologically safe practices.

        HR professionals may also not feel empowered to make decisions in their organization

based on the participants’ positional view of leadership described in their interviews. If HR

professionals feel they are unable to challenge the current universal approach of psychological

safety, then they will not be able to integrate the representation of employees with cognitive

deficits.

RQ3: How do human resource professionals describe the inclusion of employees with

cognitive deficits in DEI initiatives?

            The HR professionals interviewed in this study did not share cognitive deficits in DEI

initiatives. Three participants identified a support/resource group for disabled individuals at their

organization however, this was not specifically for individuals with cognitive deficits or invisible

disabilities. The lack of data describing the inclusion of employees with cognitive deficits in DEI

initiatives may be related to the reported move towards a universal approach to psychological

                                                                                                    90


safety. Rather than targeting specific identity groups, the participants described using empathy

and trust to build rapport and welcome all employees to promote inclusivity.

       HR professionals may also not recognize employees with cognitive deficits as an

appropriate focus for DEI initiatives, as a majority of the initiatives shared by participants

focused on race, ethnicity, and gender. Suppose HR professionals do not consider employees

with cognitive deficits as an appropriate focus for DEI initiatives. In that case, it makes sense

that they could not describe how they are represented in DEI initiatives.

       This result may also be related to a lack of experience with this population. As observed

with the participants in this study, empathy and awareness in working with individuals with

disabilities increased when there was a connection to someone with a disability. Participants

described changing the route of a site tour or adapting how they communicate with employees

based on their personal experience with someone who had a disability.

       Finally, if HR professionals do not feel empowered in their roles as leaders in their

organization, they may not feel as though they can implement new DEI initiatives or challenge

current thinking in their organizations regarding DEI. If HR professionals felt empowered as

decision-makers in their organizations and they had exposure to working with employees with

cognitive deficits, they may feel more empowered to implement novel initiatives focusing on

individuals with cognitive deficits.

                               Implications and Recommendations

       The Results and Conclusions of this study suggest several implications for current

practices in the field of HR related to expectations of the role, the challenges reported in this

study, and balancing what was described as support for the organization or for the employees.

While this seems like a false dichotomy, understanding these particular perceptions may help

                                                                                                    91


future HR professionals to address the goals of their employer while still demonstrating support

for employees. The road to solving problems begins with a conversation; this research may

represent the beginning of that conversation. By verbalizing the negative perception of their

roles, HR professionals may find ways to fulfill both responsibilities.

       This research may also affect hiring practices in HR. When an organization's leadership

understands the importance of empathy and trust between employees and HR professionals, they

may begin to hire HR professionals with that as a focus. This study strongly noted the role of

empathy as essential for developing relationships between HR and employees. Based on this

research, hiring managers should begin to transition their focus beyond education and experience

to the types of soft skills so critical to relationship building, such as active listening and empathic

perspective-taking.

       This research has highlighted the importance of personal experience and connection to

individuals with disabilities when working with employees who have invisible disabilities.

Because empathy is defined as “the ability to understand or the state of understanding others’

feelings” (Clark et al., 2018), HR professionals should educate themselves by seeking out

material in which individuals with disabilities share their feelings, such as podcasts, books,

articles, and TED talks. These materials go beyond facts and statistics of individuals with

disabilities and allow the audience to engage in perspective-taking, which has been linked to

increased empathy (Lindsey et al., 2015; Steinman, 2024). This study suggests these personal

experiences form the origins of empathy, as the audience is able to learn about the feelings

behind the experiences, which may help to create empathy (Clark et al., 2018).

       Awareness became a form of representation that helped participants build trusting

relationships with individuals with disabilities in the workplace. Participating in this research

                                                                                                     92


began a conversation with the participants, raising personal awareness that they may bring to the

workplace. Invisible disabilities are just that; invisible. If the participants from this study begin to

act with increased empathy and awareness, this may raise the perceived level of psychological

safety and allow an employee to disclose a disability they had not yet shared.

       Rather than constantly battling between support for the organization and support for the

employee, HR professionals should be able to support employees based on policies and

procedures set by the organization. In this study, participants clearly stated that psychological

safety was essential for all employees. This spoke to the importance of a culture where empathy

is the driving force it can be applied to all scenarios. This study supports the use of the universal

approach of psychological safety in order to promote an open and inclusive environment.

       As outlined in Chapter 2, Caffrey (2023) discussed the importance of active listening in

empathetic leadership. This style of leadership would be supportive to the “interactive process”

that was described by participants as their accommodations process in Chapter 4. Without having

to specifically target individuals with cognitive deficits, the culture of the organization would

already support their needs by demonstrating the empathy and trust required for a

psychologically safe environment.

Recommendations for Future Research

       Based on the findings and results from this study, further investigation into the effects of

personal beliefs and biases held by HR professionals on facilitating a psychologically safe

environment is warranted. While the results demonstrated a potential for a connection between

the personal relationship with an individual with a disability and an increase in empathy towards

employees with disabilities, this does not necessarily demonstrate a causal relationship.

                                                                                                       93


       Also of note in the findings was the difficulty for the participants to identify how

individuals with invisible disabilities were represented in DEI initiatives at their current

organizations when specifically asked by the researcher. It would be beneficial to investigate

why this is the case in a future study in order to identify if participants were not willing to

consider individuals with invisible disabilities as appropriate targets for DEI initiatives, they

were unable to due to organizational restrictions, or another reason. As we do not truly know

what the limits are that caused the participants difficulty in identifying DEI initiatives to target

individuals with invisible disabilities, this area still requires more research to better understand

what we mean by identity and inclusion.

       Further research into the connection between specific beliefs and biases held by HR

professionals and their behaviors in the workplace would benefit the fields of HR and

organizational climate/culture studies. This research would aid in designing and implementing

the suggested empathy training, as described above. There should also be further research into

the effectiveness of support/resource groups described by the participants. This further research

would also aid in the design and implementation of future support/resource groups in

professional organizations.

                                             Summary

       This phenomenological study investigated how HR professionals facilitated

psychologically safe professional environments for individuals with cognitive deficits, like those

observed in Multiple Sclerosis. 13 HR professionals were interviewed via Zoom, and answered

seven questions to distill the essential experience of these professionals in the workplace.

       The findings of the interviews led to five key results. First, a universal approach to

fostering psychological safety emerged, emphasizing the need for consistent strategies across the

                                                                                                   94


organization. Secondly, empathy and trust were identified as critical components of this

universal approach, highlighting their role in establishing a supportive and secure environment

for all employees. Third, the study revealed an evolving role for HR professionals, where

negative perceptions about HR could hinder the development of trust between employees and the

organization.

        Further findings indicated that HR professionals often adopt a positional view on

leadership, which could impact their effectiveness in promoting psychological safety. The fifth

key result noted that many HR professionals have a personal connection to disabilities, which

positively influences their ability to build trust and foster positive experiences for employees

with invisible disabilities.

        Based on these findings, implications for current practices were identified, and

recommendations for future research were made. This research can be used by current HR

professionals to better understand the perception of the field and how it continues to evolve. The

research also identified ways to better understand the current findings by continuing the research

in the connection between empathy and experience. This research highlights the need for further

studies into the relationship between the personal biases and beliefs held by HR professionals’

effect on workplace climate and culture. This research begins the conversation and investigation

into this concept; however, more in-depth, specific research is required to better understand this

phenomenon.

        It is the hope of this researcher that this study will aid HR professionals in understanding

how integral they are in supporting individuals with invisible disabilities in the workplace. This

research, however, can also be used by individuals with invisible disabilities to advocate for

themselves in the workplace by better understanding HR's role in building a psychologically safe

                                                                                                   95


workplace. Thus it is also the hope of this researcher that this study will help at least one person

with an invisible disability to advocate for themselves without fear in order to maintain their

employment and thus their independence and overall quality of life.
