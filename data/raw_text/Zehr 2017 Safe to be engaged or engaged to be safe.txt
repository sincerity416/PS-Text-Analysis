                                           Abstract

The scholarly research provides ample evidence indicating the benefits that accrue to an

organization as a result of increased levels of employee engagement and psychological

safety. A key relationship between the constructs described in the literature is one in

which psychological safety is an antecedent of employee engagement. Researchers have

also posited the reciprocal scenario in which employee engagement is an antecedent of

psychological safety. To date, the relationship between the constructs is not well

understood. Increasing the levels of employee engagement within the federal workforce

of the United States has become a priority in recent years. For management practitioners

seeking to leverage research-backed guidance, little is provided and guidance related to

the federal workforce is even more scant. This study utilized hierarchical linear

regression to quantitatively examine the relationship between employee engagement and

psychological safety in the federal workforce of the United States. Two important

findings were identified. First, in alignment with results previously reported in the

literature, psychological safety was found to be a significant predictor of employee

engagement. Second, employee engagement was determined to be a significant predictor

of psychological safety in the federal workforce. Effect sizes were calculated to

determine the magnitude of the predictive capabilities and, in both cases, the effect sizes

were small. Given the small, but statistically significant predictive effects, it is

recommended that federal managers incorporate strategies aimed to increase employee

engagement and psychological safety as part of overall strategies to increase

organizational performance.

                            Chapter 1: Introduction to the Study

                                        Background

       Employee engagement and psychological safety are constructs that represent

positive and motivated sentiments toward one’s role in the workplace (Edmondson, 1999;

Kahn, 1990). As such, high levels of employee engagement and psychological safety are

considered beneficial to the organization (Babcock-Roberson & Strickland, 2010;

Chandani, Mehta, Mall, & Khokhar, 2016; Edmondson & Lei, 2014; Frazier, Fainshmidt,

Klinger, Pezeshkan, & Vracheva, 2017; Saks, 2006; Shuck & Wollard, 2010).

Alternatively, low levels of either construct can engender deleterious organizational

effects. Disengaged employees are alleged to cost United States organizations $343

billion each year (Rivera & Flinck, 2011, p. 487). Illustrative of the negative effects

resulting from a lack of psychological safety on the organization is the tragic case of the

team of climbers that attempted to scale Mount Everest in May, 1996; five of the twenty

three team members perished and survivors characterized the culture of the expeditionary

team as unsafe for dissenters to share their perspectives (Roberto, 2002, p. 136).

       This study examines employee engagement and psychological safety within the

federal workforce of the United States. When the costs and negative effects of low levels

of employee engagement and psychological safety in the federal workforce are

considered, the importance of the research can be readily understood. For example,

Rivera and Flinck (2011) surmised that within the $343 billion in annual cost attributed to

all disengaged employees in the United States, disengaged federal employees cost United

States taxpayers $65 billion in lost productivity annually (p. 487). Likewise, the tragedy

                                                                                           2
associated with the explosion of the Columbia Space Shuttle is demonstrative of negative

effects associated with the lack of psychological safety within a federal government

agency. Employees of the National Aeronautics and Space Administration (NASA) did

not feel a sense of psychological safety with which to be able to speak up to voice

concerns regarding potential threats (Roberto, Bohmer, & Edmondson, 2006). Employee

engagement and psychological safety are topics that are as relevant in the federal

government as they are in the private sector (Rivera & Flinck, 2011).

Employee Engagement

         Employee engagement is a subject that has gained increasing attention from both

scholars and practitioners over the last 25 years (Albrecht, Bakker, Gruman, Macey, &

Saks, 2015; Bailey, Madden, Alfes, & Fletcher, 2017; Christian, Garza, & Slaughter,

2011; Shuck & Wollard, 2010; Welch, 2011). As the global marketplace evolves and

becomes increasingly competitive, the importance of employees to overall organizational

success is a topic of growing relevance (Chandani et al., 2016; Fleming, Coffman, &

Harter, 2005; Manas-Rodriguez, Alcarez-Pardo, Pecino-Medina, & Limbert, 2016).

Pursuing increased levels of employee engagement is a potential strategy for an

organizational leader attempting to harness the power of human capital to create

competitive advantage (Harter, Schmidt, & Hayes, 2002; Rich, Lepine, & Crawford,

2010).

         The organizational advantages of an engaged workforce are not limited to the

private sector. Engaged public sector federal employees of the United States government

exhibit positive organizational benefits including enhanced efficiency, improved levels of

                                                                                             3
client satisfaction, increased innovation, and less voluntary employee turnover (Marrelli,

2011; United States Office of Personnel Management, 2015). Lavigna (2011) succinctly

summarized the beneficial aspects of an engaged federal workforce in stating

“organizations that have engaged employees perform better than organizations whose

employees are not engaged” (p. 52).

       Not surprisingly, employee engagement, and the potential benefits of an engaged

workforce, are topics of growing interest within the United States federal sector as

government agencies strive to increase employee engagement levels (“Unlocking Federal

Talent,” n.d.). To support federal agencies and their leaders in attempts to increase

organizational employee engagement, the Office of Personnel Management (OPM) has

developed a dedicated website with which to share employee engagement resources,

encourage collaboration, and present best practices for federal leaders. Despite the

increased interest in federal employee workforce engagement, the majority of research on

the topic has involved the private sector (Rivera & Flinck, 2011). Given the

aforementioned positive effects of engaged federal employees, and the emphasis placed

on the topic by OPM, one can reasonably expect that increasing employee engagement

levels within the federal workforce will remain a relevant topic for the foreseeable future.

Psychological Safety

       Within the context of leveraging human capital to create organizational

advantage, psychological safety is an emerging concept (Edmondson & Lei, 2014;

Rimm-Kaufman, 2016). Edmondson (1999) described psychological safety within the

setting of a work team as an environment in which team members feel empowered to

                                                                                             4
engage in “interpersonal risk taking” (p. 354). Employees who perceive a climate

characterized by psychological safety benefit the organization through a willingness to

openly share ideas and collaborate across the enterprise without fear of repercussions and

damage to personal reputation (Carmeli & Gittell, 2009; Edmondson & Lei, 2014; Kahn,

1990). Lyu (2016) suggested psychological safety is an essential component of the

workplace and posited that humans have an “underlying psychological need for safety”

(p. 1360).

Employee Engagement and Psychological Safety

       In the conceptualization of employee engagement, Kahn (1990) identified

psychological safety as one of three conditions necessary for employee engagement

(along with psychological meaningfulness and psychological availability). Through the

establishment of an organizational culture characterized by psychological safety, the

organization may also experience increased levels of employee engagement (Christian et

al., 2011; Kahn, 1990; Lyu, 2016; May, Gilson, & Harter, 2004). Another potential

connection between employee engagement and psychological safety emerges when one

considers the proposition of Macey and Schneider (2008a) that employee engagement

may be influenced by trust, which is an antecedent of psychological safety (Edmondson

& Lei, 2014).

       In a meta-analytical review of the research related to psychological safety, Frazier

et al. (2017) identified the reciprocal relationship in which employee engagement may

influence psychological safety as an important topic for future research. Saks (2006)

utilized tenets of social exchange theory to suggest that employees with a highly-positive

                                                                                               5
relationship with their supervisor, reciprocate by being more engaged in the workplace.

Whereas, a high-quality relationship with the supervisor is a key antecedent of

psychological safety (Edmondson, 1999; Kahn, 1990), Saks (2006) provided a conceptual

basis for employee engagement as a predictor of psychological safety. Therefore, this

research study also empirically examines the potential for employee engagement to

predict psychological safety.

                                 Statement of the Problem

       The connections between employee engagement and psychological safety are

important to study for both the scholar and the practitioner. Other than the work of May

et al. (2004) and Rich et al. (2010), the literature contains a surprising lack of research

that provides quantitative validation of Kahn’s (1990) conceptualization of employee

engagement and the role of psychological safety in engagement. This study adds depth

and extends the existing body of research by seeking to quantitatively validate Kahn’s

(1990) research related to the role of psychological safety in employee engagement.

       Kahn (1990) suggested that psychological safety is a necessary condition for

employee engagement. The examination of the reverse scenario, in which employee

engagement level predicts psychological safety, presents an enthralling and unanswered

question that is also addressed in this research. For both the scholar and the practitioner,

implications of findings indicating that employee engagement and psychological safety

act in concert to produce positive effects on the organization present intriguing

ramifications for both future study and practical application.

                                                                                               6
       Illustrative of the significance of the topic is research indicating that when

employees do not feel psychologically safe, they feel as if they are being threatened

(Edmondson, 1999) and respond by displaying detrimental behaviors such as

unwillingness to take risks, lack of trust in both supervisors and peers, and increased

susceptibility to feelings of abandonment (Tiwari & Lenka, 2016). In an organization

characterized as an unsafe environment, the culture can be considered “ambiguous,

unpredictable, and threatening” (May et al., 2004, p. 15). Likewise, employees who are

not engaged exhibit a lack of enthusiasm for the organization and its mission, are less

involved, have lower productivity, and act in ways that demoralize those employees who

are engaged (Endres & Mancheno-Smoak, 2008). An understanding of the connections

between employee engagement and psychological safety is vital in assisting practitioners

seeking to avoid employee behavioral problems associated with lower levels of

psychological safety and/or employee engagement.

        Researchers know very little about the relationship between employee

engagement and psychological safety (Bailey, Madden, Alfes, & Fletcher, 2017; Lyu,

2016), and the literature is even more scant relative to results associated with the federal

workforce. For the practitioner attempting to determine organizational strategy, the

paucity of research on the topic means that little data-driven guidance is available. The

scholarly literature does, however, point to numerous beneficial organizational

consequences of psychological safety (Edmondson & Lei, 2014; Frazier et al., 2017) and

employee engagement (Babcock-Roberson & Strickland, 2010; Chandani et al., 2016;

Shuck & Wollard, 2010). Consequently, practitioners seeking to benefit from

                                                                                            7
psychological safety and employee engagement initiatives must understand the

relationship between the constructs.

       Leaders within federal government agencies are under a mandate to increase

employee engagement levels (Donovan, Corbet, Archuleta, & McLaughlin, 2014). The

research community has provided little practical information from which federal agency

leaders seeking to increase employee engagement levels may draw. For the federal

management practitioner, an implication of understanding the relationship between

federal employee engagement and psychological safety is that the knowledge may be

leveraged to create a more favorable and productive workplace climate.

       Noteworthy is the fact that the need for practical guidance on the topic of

employee engagement is not merely confined to leaders within the federal government.

Writing about the state of organizational human resource development research, Shuck

and Reio (2011) stated “as practitioners turn to scholars for tested strategies on how to

develop an engaged workforce, they are increasingly met with a gap in research to help

guide their practice” (p. 421). Further, there exists a gap between the topics examined

through scholarly research and the desire for actionable research results within the

practitioner community (Shuck & Herd, 2012; Welch, 2011). Macey and Schneider

(2008b) identified the need to bridge the gap between employee engagement scholars and

practitioners and posited one of the fundamental reasons as “scholars and practitioners

think and speak about employee engagement in different ways” (p. 79).

       In general, the topic of employee engagement in the federal workforce has been

under-researched (Jin & McDonald, 2016; Rivera & Flinck, 2011). Results from a search

                                                                                              8
of the literature using both EBSCOhost and Google Scholar indicated the availability of

only five scholarly, peer-reviewed articles related to the subject. Regarding research

encompassing the combined topics of federal employees, employee engagement, and

psychological safety, no relevant research was discovered. Adding to the body of

scholarly knowledge on the topic not only extends the existing research but also provides

compelling data for the use of federal practitioners seeking to leverage the role of

psychological safety in engendering increased employee engagement and vice-versa.

       As previously noted, Kahn (1990) proposed a linkage between employee

engagement and three psychological conditions – meaningfulness, safety, and

availability. In one of the few works researching employee engagement in the federal

workforce, the Office of Personnel Management (2015) addressed Kahn’s (1990)

psychological conditions of meaningfulness and availability, but provided very little

examination of the role of psychological safety. Therefore, of the three conditions posited

by Kahn (1990), this research addresses the gap and focus on the psychological condition

of safety within the federal workforce and its role in employee engagement.

                                   Purpose of the Study

       The purpose of this quantitative study was to gain a better understanding of the

relationship between employee engagement and psychological safety within the federal

workforce. The study provides research results with which to help bridge the gap between

scholarly research and practice in employee engagement. In doing so, one of the primary

goals of the research was to equip federal leaders with useful results to better inform the

development of effective employee engagement strategies. The problem is worthy of

                                                                                             9
research in that by gaining a better understanding of the relationship between employee

engagement and psychological safety, federal management practitioners are better

positioned to utilize empirically-developed intervention strategies to ultimately increase

employee engagement and psychological safety, thereby accruing organizational benefits.

                                     Research Design

       The extant literature contains only limited empirical research examining the

relationship between employee engagement and psychological safety (Bailey et al., 2017;

Lyu, 2016). This research utilized a quantitative research methodology to address the

need for continued research on the topic. The quantitative methodology utilized

regression analysis to determine if a model predicting federal employee engagement

based on psychological safety (see Figure 1) can be concluded. Additionally, the

reciprocal situation was examined in an effort to determine if psychological safety can be

predicted from federal employee engagement. Finally, a model in which employee

engagement and psychological safety may work in concert, as proposed by Frazier et al.

(2017), is discussed.

                                  Research Questions

        This study’s research questions are: Does psychological safety predict employee

engagement in the United States federal workforce? Does employee engagement predict

psychological safety in the United States federal workforce?

Hypotheses

        H10 : Psychological safety does not predict the employee engagement of United

States federal employees.

        H1α: Psychological safety predicts the employee engagement of United States

federal employees.

        H20 : Employee engagement does not predict the psychological safety of United

States federal employees.

        H2α: Employee engagement predicts the psychological safety of United States

federal employees.

                                     Key Definitions

Employee Engagement

        Employee engagement is defined as “the harnessing of organizational members’

selves to their work roles” (Kahn, 1990, p. 694).

Psychological Safety

        Psychological safety is defined as “feeling able to show and employ one's self

without fear of negative consequences to self-image, status, or career” (Kahn, 1990, p.

708).

                                                                                          11
Federal Employee

       Within the context of the proposed study, a federal employee is defined as a

direct, civilian employee of the United States government. As such, members of the

United States armed services and employees of government contractors are not

considered federal employees.

                                      Delimitations

       The issue of employee engagement has become rather popular with the

practitioner community as evidenced by a plethora of articles appearing within

practitioner publications (Shuck & Reio, 2011; Zigarmi, Nimon, Houson, Witt, & Diehl,

2009). This study, however, focused predominantly on peer-reviewed, scholarly journal

content. To coincide with the pioneering work of Kahn (1990) on the topic, employee

engagement literature since 1990 was emphasized. The psychological safety literature

since 1965 is highlighted to coincide with the foundational work on the topic by Schein

and Bennis (1965).

                               Chapter 2: Literature Review

                         Introduction to the Literature Review

       A review of the extant literature reveals the fact that employee engagement is not

a concept with a single, consensus theory (Meyer & Gagne, 2008). Psychological safety,

on the other hand, appears to be a study area with consensus regarding the theoretical and

practical significance of the topic (Edmondson & Lei, 2014). The literature review

identifies and discusses scholarly, peer-reviewed research on employee engagement and

psychological safety in detail and included treatment of the conceptualization, underlying

theories, and synthesis of the relevant research for both concepts. The research related to

the federal workforce was examined and the literature review concluded with a brief

review of the evolution of the research related to the relationship between psychological

safety and employee engagement.

                                 Employee Engagement

       As a research field, employee engagement has seen remarkable growth in the

recent era (Bakker, 2011; Jin & McDonald, 2016; Macey and Schneider, 2008a; Rich,

Lepine. & Crawford, 2010) yet, in assessing the body of research related to employee

engagement, Wood, Kim, and Khan (2016) determined that the field of study is still

relatively young. Despite growth in the exploration of the subject, the body of research

related to employee engagement still lacks depth, breadth, and adequate explanatory

theory (Bailey et al., 2017; Macey & Schneider, 2008a; Shuck & Wollard, 2010).

Illustrative is the lack of substantial progress in defining employee engagement and its

antecedents and outcomes noted by Zigarmi et al. (2009).

                                                                                             13
       Bailey et al. (2017) suggested that one rationale for the lack of depth in employee

engagement research is that, until recently, replication studies were rarely included in

scholarly publications within the social sciences due to editorial bias against such studies.

As such, much of the empirical research evidence related to employee engagement is

based on single studies. Wollard and Shuck (2011) noted a general lack of validation

studies within the employee engagement body of research and specifically suggested a

need for quantitative testing of antecedents to employee engagement across a variety of

organizational sectors and settings. By adding both breadth and depth to the field, this

research study is positioned to contribute to the body of work in employee engagement

research.

Conceptualization of Employee Engagement

        The foundational work on employee engagement was the research of Kahn

(1990). Kahn’s research utilized the qualitative methodology to complete two separate

studies. The work identified and defined employee engagement as “the harnessing of

organizational members’ selves to their work roles” (Kahn, 1990, p. 694) and related the

concept to psychological conditions as precursors. As previously noted, Kahn (1990)

proposed three psychological conditions of employee engagement including

psychological meaningfulness, psychological safety, and psychological availability.

        Noteworthy to an examination of the conceptualization of employee engagement

is the fact that after Kahn’s (1990) initial description of the construct, the literature does

not reflect further examination of employee engagement until the conceptual work of

                                                                                           14
Maslach, Schaufeli, and Leiter (2001) who examined employee disengagement and

concluded lack of engagement to be analogous to burnout.

         Although important follow-up research in the field occurred prior to 2008 (e.g.,

Harter et al., 2002; May et al., 2004; Maslach et al., 2001; Saks, 2006; Schaufeli,

Salanova, González-Romá, & Bakker, 2002; Sonnentag, 2003) evidence from the

literature points to 2008 as the year in which scholarly interest in employee engagement

became more widespread. For example, Wollard and Shuck (2011) noted that 2008

marked the year during which employee engagement began to be presented and discussed

at scholarly conventions. Further argument for 2008 as a critical juncture for the study of

employee engagement is the observation that of the 1,542 scholarly, peer-reviewed

articles on the construct (Wood et al., 2016, p. 329), a total of 161 were published

between 1990 and 2008 (Wollard & Shuck, 2011, p. 438). Therefore, slightly over 10%

of the research published on employee engagement was published prior to 2008.

       Providing further evidence suggesting 2008 as an important year for the study of

employee engagement is the widely-cited and pivotal treatise of Bakker, Schaufeli,

Leiter, and Taris (2008). Identified by the authors as a position paper, the treatise of

Bakker et al. (2008) provided a compendium on the state of the research of employee

engagement and examined conceptual issues, measurement, relationship of engagement

with job resources, antecedents, and correlations with performance. The authors provided

a lengthy section that laid out a proposed research agenda and a further conceptualization

of employee engagement (Bakker et al., 2008).

                                                                                                15
        Over the course of development following Kahn’s (1990) pivotal work on the

topic, researchers have described several related constructs including job engagement

(Schaufeli et al., 2002), organizational commitment (Saks, 2006), job involvement (Rich

et al, 2010), and organizational identification (Saks, 2006). Further, engagement has been

presented as the opposite of burnout (Maslach et al., 2001; Schaufeli et al., 2002). While

each of the aforementioned concepts relates to the employee’s relationship with the

organization, employee engagement is a distinct construct (Khoreva & van Zalk, 2016;

Shuck, Adelson, & Reio, 2016).

        Definition of employee engagement. Since Kahn’s (1990) conceptualization of

the term, researchers have offered varying perspectives on defining employee

engagement. Despite numerous efforts to offer a definition, there remains a lack of

consensus definition within the research community (Zigarmi et al., 2009). As previously

noted, Maslach et al. (2001) defined employee engagement as the opposite of burnout.

Schaufeli, et al. (2002) identified vigor, absorption, and dedication as three factors central

to employee engagement, which formed the basis for the authors’ proposed definition

which describes employee engagement as “a positive, fulfilling, work-related state of

mind that is characterized by vigor, dedication, and absorption” (p. 74). The definition of

Schaufeli et al. (2002) is widely cited within the research community but is not without

its detractors. Christian et al. (2011) suggested that the description of Schaufeli et al. is

more reflective of satisfaction with one’s job, employee commitment to the organization,

and burnout rather than a definition of employee engagement.

                                                                                             16
       Harter et al. (2002) proposed that employee engagement be defined as “the

individual’s involvement and satisfaction with as well as enthusiasm for work” (p. 269).

In foundational work testing antecedents and outcomes of employee engagement, Saks

(2006) defined the concept as “a distinct and unique construct that consists of cognitive,

emotional, and behavioral components that are associated with individual role

performance” (p. 602). Shuck and Wollard (2010) defined employee engagement as “an

individual employee’s cognitive, emotional, and behavioral state directed toward desired

organizational outcomes” (p. 103).

       The definition as originally presented by Kahn (1990) is the most appropriate for

this study. In seeking to understand the relationship between employee engagement and

psychological safety, the proposed study contextually aligns with Kahn’s (1990) premise

of the dependency of the employee’s psychological state on his or her current assessment

of the work environment. The notion of psychological safety and employee engagement

as adaptable depending on perception of the circumstances is inherent within the

definition proposed by Kahn (1990), thereby illustrating the importance for

organizational leadership to understand the connection between the two.

       Validation of Kahn’s results. The empirical work of May et al. (2004)

confirmed Kahn’s (1990) qualitative findings regarding the relationship between

psychological meaningfulness, psychological safety, and psychological availability and

employee engagement. May et al. (2004) found that job fit was positively associated with

psychological meaningfulness, constructive relationships with colleagues and supervisors

were associated with psychological safety, and psychological availability was positively

                                                                                          17
linked to the work experience and availability of resources. Rich et al. (2010) conducted

empirical research to validate the results of Kahn (1990) using alternate constructs

posited to be analogous to Kahn’s (1990) psychological conditions. For example, the

researchers considered perceived organizational support to be descriptive of

psychological safety (Rich et al., 2010). Rich et al. (2010) concluded that their study

empirically validated the results produced by Kahn (1990). Other than the empirical work

of May et al. (2004) and Rich et al (2010), little empirical examination of Kahn’s (1990)

findings related to psychological safety and employee engagement has been conducted

(Bailey et al., 2017; Lyu, 2016).

Employee Engagement Theory

         Describing employee engagement theory, Bailey, Madden, Alfes, and Fletcher

(2017) labeled the construct as “under-theorized” (p. 45). Macey and Schneider (2008a)

described employee engagement as a construct characterized by “a sparse and diverse

theoretically and empirically demonstrated nomological net” (p. 3). Indeed, an

examination of the extant literature indicates a diverse set of theories related to employee

engagement including role theory (Kahn, 1990), job demands-resources model

(Demerouti, Nachreiner, Bakker, & Schaufeli, 2001), and social exchange theory (Saks,

2006).

         Role theory. The grounded theory work (Creswell, 2013) of Kahn (1990) was

conceptually based on previous work of Goffman (1961) related to role theory. The

research of Goffman (1961) reported on the attachment and detachment of individuals to

their work roles. In alignment with the tenets of role theory, Kahn (1990) described

                                                                                          18
employee engagement in terms of employees expressing themselves and acting in their

work roles in accordance with their true selves. As previously noted, Kahn’s (1990)

foundational work further proposed that employee engagement, or disengagement, was a

result of the employee’s assessment of three psychological factors including:

psychological meaningfulness, psychological safety, and psychological availability. Kahn

(1990) defined psychological meaningfulness as “a feeling that one is receiving a return

on investments of one's self in a currency of physical, cognitive, or emotional energy” (p.

703). In essence, Kahn suggested that employees experience psychological

meaningfulness when they find significance in the work being performed and feel valued

by their leaders and the organization. Kahn (1990) defined psychological safety as the

employee being “able to show and employ one's self without fear of negative

consequences to self-image, status, or career” (p. 708). Kahn closely associated the

notion of trust with psychological safety in that he posited that employees who are able to

trust their coworkers and supervisor feel safer to express themselves in the work

environment. Kahn (1990) defined psychological availability as “the sense of having the

physical, emotional, or psychological resources to personally engage at a particular

moment” (p. 714). Kahn asserted that psychological availability described the readiness

of employees to engage in their work based on experienced emotions.

       In a follow- up study, Kahn (1992) provided amplification on several aspects of

personal psychology related to employee engagement and role theory. Psychological

presence was posited as a trait that would manifest itself in engagement (Kahn, 1992). To

describe psychological presence, Kahn (1992) synthesized previous organizational and

                                                                                             19
clinical research. Psychological presence was posited as being an indicator of work

motivation and the role of authenticity was explored and identified as a key factor for

psychological presence. Kahn (1992) concluded that employees gauge how much

authenticity they are willing to commit to their work environment and react accordingly.

       Job demands-resources model. The job demands-resources model is widely

used within the research community as a framework with which to describe employee

engagement (Bailey et al., 2017). According to the model, when employees are presented

with a demanding workplace characterized by increasing workload and stress, the

provision of additional resources can serve as mitigation to the stress-related effects on

the employee (Bakker & Demerouti, 2016). The job demands-resources model suggests

that when employees are granted increased resources, they are more prone to be engaged

(Bailey et al., 2017)

       Demerouti et al. (2001) established the job demands-resources model in a

foundational study designed as a means with which to address perceived shortcomings

with previous attempts to characterize and measure burnout, which they suggested was

the opposite state of engagement. Within the model, job demands are related to burnout,

specifically through exhaustion, and a lack of job resources created employee

disengagement (Demerouti et al., 2001). Demerouti et al. (2001) concluded that in the

presence of high job demands, employees experience burnout but do not necessarily

become disengaged.

       Extending the work of Harter et al. (2002) and Saks (2006) on antecedents and

outcomes of employee engagement, Bakker (2011) highlighted a practical model based

                                                                                          20
on the job demands-resources model of Demerouti et al. (2001). An essential premise of

the work of Bakker (2011) was that the proposed model provides an effective link

between scholarly research of employee engagement and practical application to the

workplace, including the use of interventions to increase employee engagement levels.

Shuck, Collins, Rocco, and Diaz (2016) examined the job demands-resources model in

the context of “privilege and power” (p. 219) and suggested that organizations seeking

increased levels of employee engagement are obliged to be cognizant of the inequality

and confront cases where privilege is exploited. A further example of practical utilization

of the job demands-resources model is an application in which linkages between

employee engagement and career are examined (Lee, Kwon, Kim, and Cho, 2016). Using

the job demands-resources model as a theoretical foundation, Lee et al. (2016) asserted

that engaged employees are committed to their career, satisfied, and less prone to career

turnover.

       Within the engagement literature, the preponderance of the research utilizes the

job demands-resources model as the theoretical basis (Bailey et al., 2017; Saks &

Gruman, 2014). Despite the wide usage of the model within the research community,

Saks and Gruman (2014) asserted that the job demands-resources model is less

theoretical in nature and more aptly described as a schema with which to classify job

demand and resources. Saks and Gruman (2014) further asserted that the job demands-

resources model is not in alignment with the pivotal work of Kahn (1990). Schaufeli

(2012) offered the perspective that the model was insufficient for examining the

“underlying psychological mechanisms” (p. 7) of employee engagement and its linkages.

                                                                                           21
Whereas this study is grounded in the foundational work of Kahn (1990) on employee

engagement and psychological safety, the job demands-resources model was not utilized

as the theoretical basis, despite its popularity with the research community (Bailey et al.,

2017).

         Social exchange theory. According to Bailey et al. (2017), social exchange

theory is second only to the job demands-resources model as a theoretical underpinning

to describe employee engagement within the research community. Homans (1958)

described interactions between individuals in the context of the exchange that occurs

when people are oriented in such a way that they exert influence on each other. Homans’

description of social exchange theory indicates the presence of reinforcing behavior in the

exchange relationship as individuals seek and give reinforcement to the other party.

Homans (1958) asserted that the behavior occurs more often in the presence of

augmented reinforcement and the assessment of the value of the behavior provided tends

to be proportional with the rewarding behavior received such that an equilibrium is

reached.

         The tenets of social exchange theory can be applied to the relationship of the

employee with the organization. In alignment with social exchange theory, an employee

enters into a social exchange relationship with the organization and will seek to maximize

personal rewards which, according to Homans (1958), can be in the form of positive

feedback. In alignment with social exchange theory, the organization that proactively

affirms employees and provides favorable sentiment can reasonably expect employees to

offer affirmative behavior in exchange (Homans, 1958).

                                                                                        22
       In research applying tenets of social exchange theory as a basis with which to

extend the work of Kahn (1990), Saks (2006) identified the manifestation of positive

employee behavior in the social exchange relationship as employee engagement. Under

the principle of reciprocity associated with social exchange theory, when employees

perceive that they are valued and appreciated by the organization, the employees respond

by applying extra effort in the form of employee engagement (Bailey et al., 2017).

       Additional support for social exchange as a theoretical underpinning of employee

engagement was presented in the quantitative work of Alfes, Truss, Soane, Rees, and

Gatenby (2013) that found support for a model in which perceived positive behavior by

managers and favorable human resource practices produced increased levels of employee

engagement as a positive outcome of the social exchange relationship. Alfes et al. (2013)

further demonstrated that the increased employee engagement produced increases in

employee task performance and innovative employee behavior.

       Ascribing the behavior to basic reciprocal tenets of employee exchange theory,

AbuKhalifeh and Som (2013) suggested that employees “repay” (p. 42) the provision of

organizational resources by choosing to become more engaged. Within the context of

social exchange theory, the nature of the relationship between the employee and the

organization indicates that as long as the parties abide by the “rules of exchange”

(AbuKhalifeh & Som, 2013, p. 42), the relationship is enriched over time. AbuKhalifeh

and Som (2013) described the reciprocal exchange between the employee and the

organization as based on resources provided by each side. When one considers the

research of AbuKhalifeh and Som (2013), an assertion can be made that employee

                                                                                         23
engagement theory based on the exchange of resources may provide theoretical similarity

between social exchange theory and the job demands-resources model.

       While social exchange theory is second only to the job demands-resources model

in terms of usage to describe employee engagement within the research community, its

usefulness is considered limited by some. Shuck, Twyford, Reio, & Shuck (2014)

asserted that social exchange theory does not provide a complete explanation of the

process of employee engagement. Shuck et al. (2014) did, however, suggest that social

exchange theory may provide a useful theoretical foundation for examining the

relationship between employee engagement and performance. The effects between

employee engagement and performance were posited as being indirectly linked to social

exchange theory through organizational cultural conditions (Shuck et al, 2014).

        Theoretical orientation for study. Social exchange theory provides the most

appropriate theoretical orientation for this study. The application of social exchange

theory to employee engagement is an extension of the foundational work of Kahn (Alfes

et al., 2013; Saks, 2006), whose conceptualization of employee engagement is a basis for

the study. Kahn’s (1990) definition of the employee engagement construct detailed the

manner in which employees “harnessed” (p. 694) themselves to their roles. Social

exchange theory describes the harnessing relationship between the employee and the

organization as dependent on the employee’s perceived interchange between the two

(Homans, 1958).

       This study examined more fully the psychological conditions related to the

exchange between the employee and the work environment. Applying social exchange

                                                                                          24
theory to employee engagement leads one to note that when employees have confidence

that the organization is providing for their physical and emotional needs, they will feel a

sense of obligation (Homans, 1958) to become more attached to their jobs and the

organization (Kahn, 1990), resulting in increased employee engagement (Kahn, 1990;

Macey & Schneider, 2008a; Saks, 2006). Also, social exchange theory describes the

relationship between the employee and the organization as dynamic and subject to

variation based on the interchange between the two (Homans, 1958).

       Within the context of the social exchange relationship, employees constantly

assess the level of support and resources received from the organization and choose a

level of engagement with the organization accordingly (May et al., 2004; Saks, 2006).

Implied within Kahn’s (1990) definition of employee engagement is the notion that

psychological safety and employee engagement can vary depending on the employee’s

assessment of the equity of his or her current personal exchange relationship with the

organization. Social exchange theory provides an explanation for the ability of

organizational leadership to manage employee engagement through the use of favorable

behavioral exchange (Alfes et al., 2013). A key premise of this study was to provide

public sector leadership practitioners with research-based interventions and management

practices with which to effect increases in psychological safety and employee

engagement.

Employee Engagement and the Organization

       Engaged employees positively impact the organization in the form of

productivity, financial performance, and behavioral benefits (Babcock-Roberson &

                                                                                           25
Strickland, 2010; Chandani et al., 2016; Harter et al., 2002; Saks, 2006; Shuck &

Wollard, 2010). The research of Harter et al. (2002) focused on a variety of business and

organizational outcomes and found employee engagement to be closely associated with

employee satisfaction. According to Shuck and Wollard (2010), the research of Harter et

al. (2002) was among the first to examine the relationship between organizational profit

and employee engagement. The practitioner-oriented research of Harter et al. (2002) also

found empirical evidence in support of the organizational value of employee engagement

in producing superior business results in the form of satisfied customers, increased

employee productivity, decreased employee turnover, and greater economic returns.

       Integrating employee engagement into human resource management practices

produces positive benefits in terms of organizational performance and employee behavior

(Albrecht et al., 2015; Anitha, 2014; Chughtai & Buckley, 2008). Human resource

management practices and positive perception of line manager behavior promotes

increased employee engagement which, in turn, produces increases in employee task

performance and innovative employee behavior (Alfes et al., 2013). Increased employee

engagement is further manifest within the workforce in the form of increased

organizational citizenship behavior (Babcock-Roberson and Strickland, 2010; Chandani

et al., 2016; Rich et al., 2010; Saks, 2006). Increases in organizational citizenship

behaviors transfer to colleagues, thereby indirectly improving overall performance

(Bakker, 2011).

       Macey and Schneider (2008b) suggested that “it is essential that researchers and

practitioners have company competitive advantage as their starting point when thinking

                                                                                          26
about employee engagement” (p. 81). Elevated levels of employee engagement have the

potential to further benefit the organization in the form of competitive advantage

(Albrecht et al., 2015; Christian et al., 2011; Macey & Schneider, 2008a; Schaufeli,

2012). Engaged employees exhibit passion and commitment for their work, not because

they are compensated to do so but by personal choice because they find work to be

stimulating and enjoyable (Schaufeli, 2012; Shuck, Rocco, & Albornoz, 2011) which, in

turn, creates a critical source of organizational competitive advantage. A key argument

established within the work of Christian et al. (2011) went beyond the connection

between employee engagement and competitive advantage to propose employee

engagement as a viable mechanism with which to also maintain firm competitive

advantage.

       While the research of Harter et al. (2002), Albrecht et al. (2015), and Christian et

al. (2011) examined employee engagement at the business unit level, increased employee

engagement is also beneficial at the individual level (Saks, 2006). Engaged employees

perform at increased levels (Anitha, 2014: Rich et al., 2011). Anitha (2014) reasoned that

the increased performance from engaged employees may be a result of positive attitudinal

factors in which the employees hold the organization and its values in high regard.

Engaged employees maintain a focus on organizational business objectives and are more

prone to collaborate with their colleagues, thereby adding benefit (Anitha, 2014).

Research also indicates that employees are less likely to leave a work environment in

which they feel highly engaged (Bhattacharya & Wright, 2005; Harter et al., 2002;

Shuck, Reio, & Rocco, 2011).

                                                                                           27
        The quantitative study of Salanova, Agut, and Peiró (2005) determined that when

job resources are readily available and obstacles are removed, employees are more

engaged, which creates a culture more conducive to increased levels of customer service,

leading to increased customer loyalty. Engaged employees exhibit a willingness to take

part in extra-role behavior by expanding their amount of personal effort (AbuKhalifeh &

Som, 2013; Macey & Schneider, 2008a). The resulting effect of the discretionary effort

of engaged employees is increased customer satisfaction (Bakker et al., 2008).

        Although an engaged workforce can have positive effects on organizational

outcomes, disengaged employees can produce quite the opposite effect (Christian et al.,

2011; Frank, Finnegan, and Taylor, 2004; Shuck & Reio, 2014, Truss, Shantz, Soane,

Alfes, & Delbridge, 2013). Disengaged employees exhibit a diminished sense of well-

being (Shuck & Reio, 2014; Truss et al., 2013) and were found to perform at lower levels

(Bakker, 2011; Shuck & Reio, 2014; Christian et al., 2011). Depending on the motivation

for pursuit, employee engagement can have either positive or negative ramifications as

the increased effort can result in employee stress in the long run (Jenkins & Delbridge,

2013). Employee engagement is a key organizational challenge as disengaged employees

are more susceptible to employee turnover which, in turn, leads to increased direct and

indirect costs that can negatively impact organizational financial performance (Frank et

al., 2004).

Antecedents of Employee Engagement

        As previously noted, psychological meaningfulness, psychological safety, and

psychological availability are identified in the literature as antecedents of employee

                                                                                          28
engagement (Kahn, 1990; May et al., 2004). Several antecedents of employee

engagement have been proposed by the research community, but researchers still have

many knowledge gaps to fill (Bailey et al., 2017; Saks, 2006; Wollard & Shuck, 2011). In

their review of the literature, Wollard and Schuck (2011) identified a need for replication

studies related to antecedents of employee engagement. Bailey et al. (2017) suggested

that the lack of depth in studies on employee engagement, and the social sciences in

general, was due to publication guidelines that, until very recently, did not encourage

replication studies. Some of the antecedents to employee engagement related to

leadership, demographic factors, time of day, telework, and other factors are examined in

this section.

        Leadership. The role of leadership has been identified as vital in fostering

employee engagement (Anitha 2014; Saks & Gruman, 2014; Schaufeli, 2012). Employee

engagement is positively related to the employee’s favorable assessment of leadership

behavior (Alfes et al., 2013) and trust in leadership (Chughtai & Buckley, 2009; Wang &

Hsieh, 2013). Charismatic leadership (Babcock-Roberson & Strickland, 2010), authentic

leadership (Wang & Hsieh, 2013), transformational leadership (Breevaart, Bakker,

Hetland, Olsen, & Espevik, 2014; Christian et al., 2011; Tims, Bakker, & Xanthopoulou,

2011), and leader-member exchange (Christian et al., 2011) have all been positively

associated with increased levels of employee engagement. Shuck et al. (2016) stated that

the organization and its leadership cannot command or create employee engagement, but

can have a positive impact by developing a culture in which employee engagement can

be achieved and sustained.

                                                                                          29
       Demographic factors. Several potential demographic antecedents to employee

engagement have been examined within the research literature. Pitt-Catsouphes and

Matz-Costa (2008) found females to have higher levels of employee engagement than

their male counterparts. Alternatively, Rigg, Sydnor, Nicely, and Day (2014) found no

significant differences related to gender. Relative to the relationship between age and

employee engagement, older employees appear to be more engaged than those who are

younger (Pitt-Catsouphes & Matz-Costa, 2008; Rigg et al., 2014; Schaufeli, 2012;

Simpson, 2009). As is the case with gender, results related to employee engagement

levels and tenure appear to be mixed (Bal, De Cooman, & Mol, 2013; Rigg et al., 2014;

Sibiya, Buitendach, Kanengoni, & Bobat, 2014).

       Time of day. Sonnentag and Kühnel (2016) empirically examined the effects of

early morning reattachment to work on employee engagement. The researchers found that

employees who were able to detach from work during non-working hours and reattach

early in the workday were highly engaged (Sonnentag and Kühnel, 2016). Further,

Sonnentag and Kühnel (2016) discovered statistical support for the notion that employees

are more engaged with their work in the morning than in the afternoon.

       Telework. Noteworthy is the negative relationship between employee

engagement and telework (Sardeshmukh, Sharma, & Golden, 2012). Employees who

telework use various technological means to work from home. Telework employees are

thought to be less engaged due to a lack of personal interaction, support, and feedback

(Sardeshmukh et al., 2012).

                                                                                            30
        Additional antecedents. Numerous other antecedents have been reported to

predict employee engagement. Job fit, psychological climate, and affective commitment

are positive predictors of employee engagement (Shuck, Reio, & Rocco, 2011). In

alignment with the job demands-resources model to explain engagement, Bakker (2011)

found that job resources and personal resources were positive predictors of employee

engagement.

        Anitha (2014) identified seven factors as predictors of employee engagement

including environment, leadership, peer relationships, training, compensation, workplace

policies, and workplace wellbeing, although a potential concern with this study lies in the

lack of detail regarding the instrument used to measure employee engagement. The

treatise indicated that an instrument was developed and tested satisfactorily for reliability,

but the specific items and methodology are not disclosed.

        In discussing antecedents, it is noteworthy that the scholarly community has

treated employee engagement “as an antecedent, mediator, moderator or outcome,

depending on the focus of the study” (Bailey et al., 2017, p. 44). Further, many of the

studies within the extant literature focus both on antecedents and outcomes of employee

engagement (Bailey et al., 2017). An increased understanding of the antecedents to

employee engagement and outcomes provides informed perspective for both the scholar

and practitioner.

                                                                                       31
Measurement of Employee Engagement

       Similar to the discussion regarding the definition of employee engagement, there

does not appear to be a consensus on the most appropriate instrument with which to

measure the construct. Schaufeli et al., (2002) introduced the Utrecht Work Engagement

Scale (UWES) as a tool for the measurement of employee engagement. While there is not

universal agreement within the scholarly community, the UWES tool is widely used

within the research community (Bailey et al., 2017; Saks & Gruman, 2014). The UWES

is not without its detractors. Saks and Gruman (2014) suggested that the UWES, as a

result of having foundational tenets within the burnout research, does not represent

employee engagement as a unique construct from burnout.

       Harter et al. (2002) leveraged the Gallup database to develop the Gallup

Workplace Audit (GWA) with which to measure employee engagement. The GWA uses

a 12-item questionnaire to measure employee engagement level. May et al. (2004)

developed the Psychological Engagement Measure, which is a 13-item questionnaire that

was designed to measure employee engagement in conjunction with their research

validating the original work of Kahn (1990). To accompany research connecting social

exchange theory to employee engagement, Saks (2006) developed the Job Engagement

and Organizational Engagement Scales. The separate scales featured six items each and

were used in testing a model of antecedents and outcomes of employee engagement

developed by Saks (2006).

       Drawing from the foundational work of Kahn (1990), Rich et al. (2010) proposed

use of the Job Engagement Measure for measuring the employee engagement construct as

                                                                                           32
originally contextualized by Kahn. The instrument developed by Rich et al. (2010)

consists of 18 items and the authors assert that the scale accurately measures the three

dimensions of employee engagement as theorized by Kahn (1990). Also claiming

alignment to the work of Kahn (1990), Soane, Truss, Alfes, Shantz, Rees, and Gatenby

(2012) developed the Intellectual, Social, and Affective (ISA) Engagement Scale as a tool

with which to measure employee engagement. Using dimensions of engagement

including intellectual, social, and affective elements, the researchers suggested that their

work was a continuation of Kahn’s (1990) foundational work on employee engagement.

       Kumar and Pansari (2015) took a decidedly practitioner-based approach in

combining an extensive review of the literature with a qualitative study to develop a

proposed employee engagement scorecard for use by organizational leadership. In the

qualitative study, manager responses to a series of queries about organizational strategies

for employee engagement were examined. Factors observed to affect employee

engagement included satisfaction, commitment, identification, loyalty and performance

(Kumar and Pansari, 2015).

       Expressing the perspective that previous measurement scales focused only on

various aspects of employee engagement, Shuck et al. (2016) developed the Employee

Engagement Scale and asserted that the instrument was a broad tool to measure employee

engagement. Shuck et al. (2016) argued that employee engagement should be considered

a separate construct from job engagement and suggested “job engagement describes the

degree to which a person is engaged with his or her job only, whereas employee

engagement focuses toward the fuller experiences of employees active roles within their

                                                                                          33
work” (p. 4). The Employee Engagement Scale consists of three elements including

cognitive, emotional, and behavioral and is grounded in the work of Kahn (1990). As

such, the Employee Engagement Scale is well-suited for this research study.

Alternate Perspectives of Employee Engagement

        A more critical approach to employee engagement was adopted in treatises from

Little and Little (2006) and Macey and Schneider (2008a). Little and Little (2006)

questioned the need for employee engagement as a construct. As the basis for their

argument, the researchers contended that employee engagement was not different from

other established fields of study such as job satisfaction or organizational commitment

(Little & Little, 2006). The authors suggested that if employee engagement is indeed

found to be a term that is redundant with other research terms, the research community

should reconsider its approach. However, Little and Little (2006) also recommended that

further development and definition is needed and suggested that, if indeed research areas

that are not redundant are described, employee engagement research should most

certainly be retained as a field of study.

        Macey and Schneider (2008a) suggested that the employee engagement research

community must provide clarity on the subject by “untangling the jangle” (p. 5). The

authors described employee engagement as an observed behavior and drew a distinction

between the state and trait engagement perspectives of employee engagement (Macey &

Schneider, 2008a). The authors further suggested that most of the existing measurement

instruments failed to adequately operationalize employee engagement (Macey &

Schneider, 2008a).

                                                                                           34
       Using a case study example from the United States Postal Service, Endres and

Mancheno-Smoak (2008) pointed to confusion resulting from attempts to distinguish

employee engagement from employee focus. Suggesting that employee engagement was

a “buzz term,” Endres and Mancheno-Smoak (2008) asserted that the lack of a consensus

definition for the construct was a contributing factor to difficulties bridging the gap

between scholarly work and the practitioner. The fact that numerous consultants and

practitioners are engaged in the commercialization and marketing of employee

engagement interventions within the corporate marketplace in 2017 could lead one to

potentially argue that employee engagement is indeed still a buzz term.

Federal Employee Engagement

       The importance of increasing the employee engagement levels of the federal

workforce is indicated by the stated objective of the previous White House administration

to increase federal employee engagement to 67% (Donovan et al., 2014). Despite the

emphasis by the White House on employee engagement in the federal workforce, the

extant literature contains little research related to employee engagement and the public

sector (Jin and McDonald, 2016). In an examination of the research literature, Jin and

McDonald (2016) noted a single article related to federal workforce employee

engagement. The single article identified by Jin and McDonald (2016) contained a

narrow focus on sexual minorities in the federal workforce (Jin and Park, 2016) and

found that employee engagement among lesbian, gay, bisexual, and transgender (LGBT)

federal employees influenced job satisfaction less strongly than for non-LGBT federal

employees.

                                                                                            35
         Federal agencies differ from private sector. Leaders in federal agencies face

challenging circumstances and constraints that differentiate them from their counterparts

in the private sector (Seidle, Fernandez, & Perry, 2016). In recent years, federal agency

managers attempting to keep employees engaged and committed to the mission of the

organization have had to deal with budget uncertainties, pay freezes, furlough, pressure to

increase constituent services without being provided additional resources, and

competition from the private sector to hire and retain employees (Jin & McDonald, 2016;

Lavigna, 2010). The culture within federal agencies is centered on placing mission

success as the top priority, even when faced with declining organizational resources

(Rivera & Flinck, 2011).

         Federal agencies differ from their private sector counterparts in terms of the

measurement of organizational performance in that federal agencies do not have a

shareholder/profit motive and are not designed to pursue increased market share

(Lavigna, 2011). The unique nature of federal agency operation (Lavigna, 2011; Trahant,

2006; Seidle et al., 2016) indicates a need to develop employee engagement strategies in

a manner somewhat different than what might be utilized in the private sector (Lavigna,

2011).

         Government agency research on employee engagement. Studies on employee

engagement within the federal workforce have been conducted primarily by government

agencies. In separate studies, the Merit Systems Protection Board (2008) and the Office

of Personnel Management (2015) examined employee engagement within the federal

workforce. The Merit System Protection Board (2008) study determined a positive

                                                                                            36
relationship between an employee’s supervisory status and the level of employee

engagement. The researchers concluded that supervisors in the federal workforce were

more highly engaged than those who were not supervisors.

       The Merit Systems Protection Board (2008) also examined characteristics of

agencies with higher levels of employee engagement in comparison to those with lower

engagement. The research concluded that agencies with higher levels of employee

engagement experienced fewer average employee sick days than those with lover levels

of employee engagement (Merit Systems Protection Board, 2008). The impact of the cost

and missed work related to absenteeism can create a major influence on efficiency when

multiplied by the entire federal workforce (Marrelli, 2011), thereby providing further

compelling rationale for initiatives designed to increase employee engagement within the

federal workforce.

       The research study of the Office of Personnel Management (2015) was informed

from a review of the extant literature by a working group consisting of agency

researchers. A product of the literature review was a proposed model of federal employee

engagement characterized by antecedents including job characteristics, organizational

climate, and personal characteristics (Office of Personnel Management, 2015). Beneficial

outcomes of an engaged federal workforce identified in the proposed model were

productivity, retention, job satisfaction, innovation, discretionary effort, and enhanced

customer service (Office of Personnel Management, 2015). Referring to employee

engagement within the federal workforce, the Office of Personnel Management (2015)

defined the construct as “The employee’s sense of purpose that is evident in their display

                                                                                          37
of dedication, persistence, and effort in their work or overall attachment to their

organization and its mission” (p. 1).

         The work of the Office of Personnel Management (2015) identified the

importance of leadership, direct supervision, and intrinsic work experience as key

antecedents of federal workforce employee engagement. The Office of Personnel

Management (2015) further asserted that questions within the Federal Employee

Viewpoint Survey (FEVS) that related to agency leadership and direct supervision

presented measures of organizational climate, while questions related to intrinsic work

experience presented an indicator of job fit. In identifying antecedents of federal

employee engagement, the Office of Personnel Management (2015) work appears to link

to two of Kahn’s (1990) three antecedents as further described by the work of May et al.,

(2004). The inclusion of factors related to job fit is descriptive of psychological

meaningfulness as described by May et al. (2004). Likewise, the inclusion of factors

related to organizational climate is descriptive of psychological availability as presented

by May et al. (2004). While the Office of Personnel Management (2015) described the

importance of two of Kahn’s (1990) three psychological antecedents, the role of

psychological safety was not directly included as a factor despite qualitative evidence

(Kahn, 1990) and quantitative evidence (May et al., 2004) that it would be appropriate to

do so.

         The Office of Personnel Management (2015) also noted beneficial results in

association with increased levels of federal employee engagement including decreased

employee attrition from voluntary turnover, increased innovative behavior, and higher

                                                                                         38
levels of efficiency. The study also corroborated the earlier findings of the Merit Systems

Protection Board (2008) related to employee engagement and absenteeism (Office of

Personnel Management, 2015).

       Measurement of federal employee engagement. Employee engagement levels

within the federal workforce are measured using an instrument developed by the Office

of Personnel Management (2015) consisting of responses to 15 questions included as a

part of the yearly Federal Employee Viewpoint Survey. The instrument is referred to as

the Employee Engagement Index (EEI) (Office of Personnel Management, 2015). The

Federal Employee Viewpoint Survey is a series of 84 items, primarily consisting of

questions based on a five-point Likert type scale, ranging from one (Strongly Disagree) to

five (Strongly Agree). The EEI for a federal agency is calculated based on the average

percentage of employees responding with either a four (Agree) or five (Strongly Agree)

on the EEI questions. The EEI is divided into three component areas, each represented by

five questions within the FEVS. The component areas are leaders lead, supervisor, and

intrinsic work experience. (Office of Personnel Management, 2015). The Office of

Personnel Management (2015) asserts that the EEI can more readily be described as a

tool with which to measure antecedents of employee engagement as opposed to actual

employee engagement levels.

       Synthesis of public sector employee engagement research. Lavigna (2010)

highlighted the unique factors related to challenges with federal employee engagement.

Highlights from the Federal Human Capital Survey were utilized as background for the

author’s assertions related to the importance of federal employee satisfaction and

                                                                                          39
employee engagement levels (Lavigna, 2010). Lavigna (2010) identified the role of

leadership as being critical to the successful efforts in increasing federal employee

engagement and satisfaction levels.

       Marrelli (2011) referenced two studies performed by the U.S. Merit Systems

Protection Board and described employee engagement within the federal workforce.

Drawing from the literature, Marrelli (2011) discussed research regarding beneficial

outcomes of employee engagement on organizational performance, noting that engaged

federal employees produce positive organizational benefits including enhanced

efficiency, improved levels of client satisfaction, increased innovation, and less voluntary

employee turnover. Marrelli (2011) identified the role of leadership in engendering

employee communication and identified a striking contrast between agencies scoring

higher and those scoring lower in employee engagement.

       In a quantitative study that included public and private sector employees in Israel,

Vigoda-Gadot, Eldor, and Schohat (2013) posited that employee engagement was a

distinct construct when compared with similar constructs such as affective commitment

and job involvement. Vigoda-Gadot et al. (2013) also explored differences in engagement

levels between public and private sector employees. A third issue examined was the

difference in engagement levels between public sector employees and managers (Vigoda-

Gadot et al., 2013). Findings indicated that public sector employees had higher

engagement levels and the engagement levels of public sector managers were higher than

those for employees (Vigoda-Gadot et al., 2013).

                                                                                            40
       Jin and McDonald (2016) performed a quantitative examination of the role of

supervisory support in the engagement of local and state government employees. The

work was grounded in social exchange theory and organizational support theory and

perceived organizational support was posited as a mediator between supervisory support

and employee engagement (Jin & McDonald, 2016). The researchers concluded that

perceived organizational support plays a mediating role in the engenderment of employee

engagement through supervisory actions (Jin & McDonald, 2016).

                                   Psychological Safety

       Starting in the 1990s and continuing to the present, an increased interest in

psychological safety and its role within organizations has developed (Edmondson & Lei,

2014). Edmondson and Lei (2014) asserted that the study of psychological safety is

undergoing a “renaissance” (p. 23). The reason for the interest is, in part, the knowledge

that psychological safety “facilitates the willing contribution of ideas and actions to a

shared enterprise” (Edmondson & Lei, 2014, p. 23).

Conceptualization of Psychological Safety

       Psychological safety was first described in the foundational work of Schein and

Bennis (1965). The original work conceptualized psychological safety in terms of

managing and decreasing the personal risk which is brought about by change and

ambiguous situations (Schein & Bennis 1965). According to Edmondson and Lei (2014),

after the initial work of Schein and Bennis (1965), psychological safety research was

sparse until the work of Kahn (1990) sparked a renewed interest in the subject. Kahn

(1990) examined psychological safety in two qualitative studies which were also

                                                                                            41
foundational to the employee engagement research. Summarizing the body of research on

psychological safety, Edmondson and Lei (2014) chronicled trends in research on the

topic and identified three research themes including individual, group, and organizational

level psychological safety.

       The research of Edmondson (1999) is frequently cited within the psychological

safety literature and can arguably be considered foundational work within the field.

Edmondson (1999) extended Kahn’s psychological safety construct to develop a

definition for team psychological safety. In establishing the concept of team

psychological safety, Edmondson (1999) focused on organizational team learning and

developed evidence for the importance of trust within the team. Edmondson (1999) found

support for team psychological safety as a component of individual comfort with risk-

taking and an overall sense of trust within the group. The author posited that individual

and team trust may be foundational elements necessary for the establishment of team

psychological safety (Edmondson, 1999).

       Distinct construct from employee engagement. The concepts of psychological

safety and employee engagement bear similarities in that both are representative of

positive sentiment toward the workplace (Frazier et al., 2017). The concepts are,

however, distinct in that employee engagement refers to the employee’s perception and

attitude toward his or her role in the workplace (Kahn, 1990). Psychological safety, on

the other hand, refers to the employee’s perception of the workplace climate in the

broader context, and whether the climate is conducive for risk-taking (Carmeli & Gittell,

2009; Edmondson, 1999).

                                                                                            42
         Definition of psychological safety. Unlike the lack of consensus noted with the

definition of employee engagement, the research community appears to have reached

consensus regarding the definition of psychological safety. Kahn (1990) defined

psychological safety as “feeling able to show and employ one's self without fear of

negative consequences to self-image, status, or career” (Kahn, 1990, p. 708).

Edmondson (1999) defined team psychological safety as “a shared belief that the team is

safe for interpersonal risk taking” (p. 354). A review of the extant literature indicates

consensus on the use of Kahn’s (1990) definition relative to psychological safety with

individuals and Edmondson’s (1999) definition in reference to psychological safety in

teams.

Psychological Safety Theory

         The formative theoretical work on psychological safety was performed by Schein

and Bennis (1965) whose previous work in behavioral change theory influenced their

development of the construct. As with the theoretical development of employee

engagement, the foundational work of Kahn (1990) was instrumental in theory related to

psychological safety. As previously identified, Kahn’s (1990) research was grounded in

role theory. The job demands-resources model (Demerouti et al., 2001) and social

exchange theory (Homans, 1958) are among theories that can be applicable to provide an

underlying theoretical basis for psychological safety.

         Job demands-resources model. The majority of the research using the job

demands-resources model has involved individual constructs, such as employee

engagement (Idris, Dollard, & Tuckey, 2015). Idris et al. (2015) utilized the job demands-

                                                                                        43
resources model to extend previous work on organizational psychosocial safety climate

and examine the relationship of employee learning opportunities with psychosocial safety

climate and employee engagement, linking the results to organizational performance

outcomes. Idris et al. (2015) empirically identified linkages between psychosocial safety

climate and employee engagement, and psychosocial safety climate and performance

with employee engagement as a mediator. The researchers found support for the basic

premise of the job demands-resources model which states that increased resources

enhance employee engagement (Idris et al., 2015). The role of psychological safety was

found to influence the individual employee and organizational motivation pathway of the

job demands-resources model (Idris et al., 2015).

       Social exchange theory. As previously discussed in relation to employee

engagement, social exchange theory describes a reciprocal relationship between the

employee and the organization in which exchange occurs (Homans, 1958). Framed by the

tenets of social exchange theory, when the organization provides an environment deemed

by the employee to be psychologically safe, the employee will reciprocate with a

willingness to accept organizational change (Rimm-Kaufman, 2016). Applying tenets of

social exchange theory to the relationship between psychological safety and employee

engagement in alignment with the research of Kahn (1990), when the organization

provides a culture characterized by psychological safety, the employee can be expected to

reciprocate by becoming engaged with his or her work role. In this manner, social

exchange theory becomes relevant to the practical consideration of the manner in which

the organization and the employees can both receive benefits through increased

                                                                                            44
psychological safety. Frazier et al. (2017) suggested that, within the context of the

organization, “the entirety of the social exchange system influences psychological safety”

(p. 119).

Psychological Safety and the Organization

        When employees assess the organizational climate as psychologically safe,

organizational benefits are produced through employee behaviors that promote a

productive work environment (Baer & Frese, 2003; Brown & Leigh, 1996; Edmondson,

1999). Discussing the field of research related to psychological safety, Edmondson and

Lei (2014) suggested “a central theme in research on psychological safety – across the

decades and levels of analysis – is that it facilitates the willing contribution of ideas and

actions to a shared enterprise” (p. 24). The beneficial behavior exhibited by employees

results from the assessment that “people are comfortable being themselves” (Edmondson,

1999 p. 354).

        Employee-level benefits. Benefits at the employee level as a result of

psychological safety include employee cooperation (Baer & Frese, 2003; Walumbwa &

Schaubroeck, 2009), job involvement (Brown & Leigh, 1996), willingness to accept

change (Baer & Frese, 2003), employee engagement (Kahn, 1990; May et al., 2004), role

clarity (Brown & Leigh, 1996), creativity (Kark & Carmeli, 2009; Zhou and Pan, 2015),

freedom of self-expression (Brown & Leigh, 1996; Carmeli, Sheaffer, Binyamin, Reiter-

Palmon, & Shimoni, 2014), and increased employee comfort with taking interpersonal

risk (Edmondson, 1999). Edmondson (1999) identified a positive association between

team learning behavior and psychological safety. Employees that experience

                                                                                              45
psychological safety are interested in the realization of their potential and express self-

confidence in the form of a desire to pursue continued personal and professional growth

(Carmeli et al., 2014). When employees sense an increased level of psychological safety,

they respond with increased use of their voices to effect group collaboration and

information sharing (Kahn, 1990; May et al., 2004; Walumbwa & Schaubroeck, 2009).

         Organizational-level benefits. Brown and Leigh (1996) noted that employees

who feel psychologically safe respond with “greater job involvement and commitment of

time and energy in the work of the organization (p. 364). Psychological safety benefits

the organization through increased performance (Baer & Frese, 2003; Brown & Leigh,

1996; Edmondson, 1999). Building on the foundational works of Kahn (1990) and

Edmondson (1999), Baer and Frese (2003) found that psychological safety climate was a

significant predictor of both organizational goal attainment and change in return on

assets. Organizations that are characterized by a culture of psychological safety are better

able to effect organizational initiatives that may be unpopular with employees due to the

fact that employees feel safe to express themselves and voice their concerns (Edmondson,

1999).

Antecedents of Psychological Safety

         Several antecedents to psychological safety have been identified in the research,

but “their relative importance remains unclear” (Frazier et al., 2017, p. 114). Antecedents

to psychological safety include supportive relationships with co-workers (Kahn, 1990;

Rothman & Welsh, 2013), self-consciousness (Rothman & Welsh, 2013), resources

(Edmondson, 1999), organizational norms and values (Kahn, 1990), climate of diversity

                                                                                             46
(Singh, Winkel, and Selvarajan, 2013), and access to information (Edmondson, 1999).

The presence of trust, both in leadership and co-workers, was expressed as an important

antecedent of psychological safety in the pivotal works of Kahn (1990) and Edmondson

(1999).

          Leadership. An important antecedent to psychological safety is the role of the

employee’s leader (Nembhard & Edmondson, 2006). Kahn’s (1990) research asserted the

important role of management support to psychological safety. In their validation of

Kahn’s (1990) results, May et al. (2004) corroborated the importance of the relationship

between the employee and leadership on psychological safety. Further, of the antecedents

to psychological safety examined, May et al. (2004) determined the relationship with

supervision to be the strongest. Indicating support for the important role of leadership,

Edmondson, Higgins, Singer, and Weiner (2016) found that employee perceptions of

leadership effectiveness were positively related to psychological safety.

          The act of inclusiveness, as manifested by the words and actions of the supervisor

designed to elicit employee open and honest feedback, can serve to promote a culture of

psychological safety (Nembhard & Edmondson, 2006). Shuck et al. (2016) pointed to the

important role of organizational leadership in asserting that “Work environments that are

unable to provide protective boundaries for employees can be experienced as threatening,

intimidating places of work” (p. 211). When leaders create a culture in which employees

are able to take a risk and fail without losing standing, employees exhibit creativity and

dedication to their work (Brown & Leigh, 1996).

                                                                                           47
       Leaders are afforded the opportunity to control several factors that can foster

psychological safety. Leaders play a vital role in creating positive relationships with their

subordinates, which can result in increased psychological safety (Baer & Frese, 2003;

Carmeli et al., 2014). Leadership styles such as transformational leadership (Carmeli et

al., 2014) and authentic leadership (Walumbwa and Schaubroeck, 2009) promote

psychological safety among their subordinates. In their study of authentic leadership,

Walumbwa and Schaubroeck (2009) asserted that the example and actions of ethical

leaders promotes the opportunity for employees to voice constructive perspectives related

to both ethical issues and work processes. Further, the set of human resource procedures

utilized by the organization, typically controlled by leadership, was demonstrated to be an

antecedent of psychological safety (Walumbwa and Schaubroeck, 2009).

       While leadership can play a positive role in creating psychological safety, the

potential exists for psychological safety to be negatively impacted by hierarchy

(Nembhard & Edmondson, 2006). Nembhard and Edmondson (2006) determined that

when supervisors are afforded increased social status and authority, team psychological

safety is inhibited. Of note is the finding that even a small increase in self-perceived

status of the employee produced increased psychological safety (Nembhard &

Edmondson, 2006). Individuals in higher- level roles assess organizational psychological

safety as greater than those in lower-ranking roles (Edmondson et al., 2016).

Measurement of Psychological Safety

       As a means with which to test the employee engagement model proposed by

Kahn (1990), May et al. (2004) developed a three question Likert-style survey with

                                                                                           48
which to measure psychological safety. The questions were designed to determine

“whether the individual felt comfortable to be themselves at work or whether there was a

threatening environment at work” (May et al., 2004, p. 21). The psychological safety

score is calculated by averaging the participant responses on the three items. The three

items are:

       1) I’m not afraid to be myself at work.

       2) I am afraid to express my opinions at work.

       3) There is a threatening environment at work.

Interestingly, the psychological safety instrument includes one positively oriented

question and two that are reversed (May et al., 2004). While May et al. (2004) were able

to successfully deploy their psychological safety instrument in validating the work of

Kahn (1990), the scale has occasionally produced poor reliability scores for other

researchers (e.g. Chikoko, Buitendach, & Kanengoni, 2014; Rothmann & Welsh, 2013).

       Edmondson (1999) developed a seven-item questionnaire, which was successfully

deployed in her research. The scale developed by Edmondson (1999) was conceived in

support of a mixed-methods study in which a qualitative examination was followed by

quantitative validation and further qualitative examination. A review of the literature

finds that a preponderance of researchers seeking to measure psychological safety either

utilize the instrument developed by May et al. (2004) or Edmondson (1999).

       Relationship between Employee Engagement and Psychological Safety

       In his foundational research on employee engagement, Kahn (1990) identified

psychological safety as an antecedent of employee engagement. Aside from the work of

                                                                                          49
May et al. (2004) to validate Kahn’s (1990) study, a relatively small amount of research

examining the relationship between employee engagement and psychological safety is

noted in the extant literature.

Synthesis of Literature Combining Employee Engagement and Psychological Safety

        The foundational research of Kahn (1990) combined employee engagement and

psychological safety. May et al. (2004) conducted a validation study and found empirical

support for Kahn’s (1990) assertion of the positive relationship between the employee

engagement and psychological safety. Aside from these studies, Lyu (2016) suggested

that researchers know very little about the relationship between the constructs. Relative to

the federal workforce, the relationship between employee engagement and psychological

safety is a topic upon which the extant literature is silent.

        Dollard and Bakker (2010) used the term “psychological safety climate” (PSC) to

refer to facets of the organizational culture related to employee psychological health and

safety. Using a quantitative approach, the researchers based their work on the job

demands-resources model to examine a theorized model in which organizational

leadership’s impact on job demands and resources affected PSC and employee

engagement. Measurement of employee engagement was accomplished through using the

UWES instrument, while psychological safety was embedded within a four-question

instrument designed to measure PSC. Interestingly, Dollard and Bakker (2010) provided

a slightly different understanding of psychological safety than was posited by Kahn

(1990). Dollard and Bakker (2010) suggested PSC as an antecedent of psychological

meaningfulness, safety, and availability. The study indicated statistical support for a

                                                                                          50
model in which PSC is a basic organizational cultural condition affecting employee

engagement and psychological welfare (Dollard and Bakker, 2010).

       Chen, Zang, and Vogel (2011) mixed conflict management theory related to task

and relationships with work engagement and empirically measured effects on knowledge

sharing. More specifically, Chen et al. (2011) examined task and relationship conflict

effects on Kahn’s psychological antecedents of engagement (meaningfulness, safety,

availability) and then measured the effects of the resulting employee engagement on the

employee’s participation in knowledge transfer. Employee engagement was measured

using the scale established by May et al. (2004). Psychological meaningfulness, safety,

and availability were also measured using the scales developed May et al. (2004). Results

of the study indicated that conflict in relationships led to decreased levels of

psychological meaningfulness, safety, and availability, and employee engagement which,

in turn led to decreased knowledge sharing within the organization (Chen et al., 2011).

       Utilizing the job demand/resources model framework, Law, Dollard, Tuckey, and

Dormann, (2011) examined psychological safety climate (PSC) to determine its

relationship with several organizational concepts, one of which was employee

engagement. The quantitative study found empirical support for a negative relationship

between PSC and workplace bullying (Law et al., 2011). Law et al. (2011) found positive

empirical support for the relationship between PSC and work-related rewards. Relative to

employee engagement, Law et al. (2011) concluded that when the relationship between

PSC and rewards was evident, increased levels of employee engagement resulted.

                                                                                          51
        The research of Shuck, Rocco, and Albornoz (2011) utilized the qualitative

tradition to examine employee perspectives regarding employee engagement and the

psychological factors identified by Kahn (1990). Emergent themes included the important

role of coworkers and supervisors in helping employees develop mutually beneficial

relationships in the workplace (Shuck et al., 2011). In alignment with the foundational

work of Kahn (1990), the researchers found that psychological safety, meaningfulness,

and availability within the workplace culture were important factors in developing an

engaged workforce.

        Using a study design focused on antecedents of employee engagement among

employees in Namibia, Rothmann and Welsh (2013) emphasized the role of Kahn’s

(1990) psychological conditions. Multiple regression analysis was utilized to predict

effects of psychological meaningfulness, safety, and availability on employee

engagement (Rothman & Welsh, 2013). Some of the other antecedents measured were

work-role fit, relationship with supervisor, and organizational support. Interestingly,

Rothman and Welsh (2013) found that their results for psychological safety did not

demonstrate sufficient internal reliability as measured by Cronbach’s Alpha, so it was not

included in the results.

        Chikoko, Buitendach, and Kanengoni (2014) empirically examined employee

engagement levels within a South African University based on psychological conditions

and job characteristics. Multiple regression was used in an attempt to predict work

engagement based on psychological dimensions and job characteristics (Chikoko et al.,

2014). While Chikoko et al. (2014) were able to draw conclusions related to work

                                                                                              52
engagement and psychological meaningfulness and availability, they were unable to test

the relationship with psychological safety due to lack of reliability noted in the scale as

measured by Cronbach’s alpha.

       In a recent quantitative study examining organizational justice, psychological

safety, and employee engagement within the Chinese teaching community, Lyu (2016)

examined the relationship between perceived organizational justice and employee

engagement with psychological safety proposed as a mediator in the relationship. Lyu

(2016) identified a positive association between organizational justice and employee

engagement and found empirical support to indicate that psychological safety partially

mediated the relationship.

       Tiwari and Lenka (2016) studied employees who had survived corporate

downsizing and examined individual psychological safety and its relationship with

employee engagement. Kahn’s (1990) definitions for psychological safety and employee

engagement were operationalized in the study (Tiwari & Lenka, 2016). Utilizing a review

of the extant literature as a basis, Tiwari and Lenka (2016) proposed a framework with

which organizations can increase levels of employee engagement through increased

psychological safety. Antecedents of psychological safety described in the study included

the chance to share information, continuous learning, and the ability to act as an

entrepreneur (Tiwari and Lenka, 2016).

Similarity in Antecedents

       Pertinent to the examination of the relationship between employee engagement

and psychological safety is the similarity in some of the antecedents. Dollard and Bakker

                                                                                             53
(2010) proposed psychological safety climate as an antecedent of both employee

engagement and psychological safety. Other similarities in antecedents include trust in

leadership (Chughtai & Buckley, 2011; Edmondson, 1999; Wang & Hsieh, 2013) and the

role of leadership (Edmondson, 1999; Wang & Hsieh, 2013). While sharing similarities

related to antecedents and positive organizational sentiment, employee engagement and

psychological safety are separate constructs (Frazier et al., 2017).

           Employee Engagement as an Antecedent to Psychological Safety

       The research literature identifies psychological safety as an antecedent to

employee engagement (Kahn, 1990; May et al., 2004). Christian et al. (2011) suggested

the possibility that employee engagement antecedents may actually increase in the

presence of elevated employee engagement and noted “it is possible that reverse or

reciprocal causality is an alternative explanation for the relations between engagement

and some factors in our model” (p. 121) and called for further research on the topic.

Building on the assertions of Christian et al. (2011) regarding reciprocity, the potential

for employee engagement to operate as an antecedent to psychological safety presents a

valid research area for exploration. Frazier et al. (2017) identified the examination of

employee engagement as an antecedent of psychological safety with “both having the

ability to influence each other positively” (p. 148) as an important area for future research

with relevance to researchers from both communities.

                                         Summary

       In summary, the literature review provided a detailed review of the

conceptualization and underlying theory related to employee engagement and

                                                                                                54
psychological safety, as separate constructs and in combination with each other. The case

of employee engagement in the federal workforce was also described. Detailed support

for the importance of researching the relationship between employee engagement and

psychological safety in the federal workforce was presented and the importance of

implications for the practitioner as a result of the study was established.

                                        Contributions

        The study contributes to the existing body of research by adding to the body of

scholarly knowledge related to psychological safety and employee engagement, both

separately and collectively. Additionally, the study addresses a gap in the literature

related to employee engagement within the federal workforce, which is a topic on which

the research literature has been mainly silent. This research helps to fill the gap and is

important as federal management practitioners seeking to increase employee engagement

in the federal workforce will benefit from the application of knowledge of the

mechanisms through which psychological safety impacts employee engagement and vice-

versa. It is further asserted that the study results are generalizable to other public sector

organizations including local, state, and federal agencies. While the federal government

workplace environment is different from the private sector in several ways, it is also

anticipated that portions of the study results may be generalizable to the private sector.

                                         Conclusion

        The relationship between employee engagement and psychological safety in the

federal workforce presents a worthy topic of further research, the results of which are

timely and relevant to the practitioner community of federal agency leaders attempting to

                                                                                           55
increase employee engagement levels within their respective organizations. It is

anticipated that the results of this research study will provide useful information with

which to craft and implement employee engagement strategies within the federal

workforce.

                               Chapter 3: Research Method

                                      Chapter Introduction

       This section will provide a description of the study methodology. Following a

brief review of the problem being investigated and the purpose for doing so, the research

design will be discussed. Included will be an explanation of the rationale for the

methodological approach as well as description of the techniques for executing the

research and analyzing the results.

                                       Research Design

       This research utilized a quantitative correlational approach to examine employee

engagement and psychological safety. The study was designed to provide results in the

form of empirical data to describe the relationship between employee engagement and

psychological safety. The quantitative correlational method is well-suited for the

examination of relationships between variables (Hair, Black, Babin, & Anderson, 2010;

Joyner, Rouse, & Glatthorn, 2013) and was, therefore, determined to be suitable for this

study. A quantitative correlational study is appropriate when the researcher wishes to

predict the value of a variable based on levels of other variables or combinations thereof

(Burns & Burns, 2013; Joyner et al., 2013) and multiple regression analysis techniques

can be leveraged to determine if statistically significant relationships between the

variables exist (Burns & Burns, 2013; Field, 2009; Hair et al., 2010). Further, the

quantitative approach is appropriate when the researcher’s interest extends beyond

empirical understanding to a desire and intent to generalize the results to a broader

population (Swanson & Holton, 2005).

                                                                                           57
       The research design in this study seeks to develop descriptive data about a

relationship based on existing conditions and associations between variables, therefore

the research can be considered non-experimental (Swanson & Holton, 2005). In essence,

the study measures psychological safety conditions that are already existent to determine

their effect on employee engagement and vice-versa. Burns and Burns (2013) asserted

that quantitative studies that involve prediction are actually an extension of correlational

research into regression techniques. The research utilizes multiple regression analysis

(Field, 2009; Hair et al., 2010) to determine if a predictive model of the relationship

between employee engagement and psychological safety can be determined from the

conditions identified in the data.

       Further support for the use of the quantitative method in this study can be found

in the method of data collection. The research design utilized a survey instrument for data

collection. Quantitative research emphasizes measurement and is useful when a survey

instrument is utilized for data collection (Gelo, Braakmann, & Benetka, 2008; Joyner,

Rouse, & Glatthorn, 2013). Joyner et al. (2013) asserted that the analysis of data

developed as a result of the administration of surveys can be readily accomplished

through the use of quantitative techniques, which are well-suited for interpreting

measurements of variables.

                           Research Questions and Hypotheses

       The research focuses on employees of the United States government and explores

the relationship between employee engagement and psychological safety. Two research

questions and an associated hypothesis for each was developed for use in the study.

                                                                                           58
Research Question One

       Does psychological safety predict employee engagement in the United States

federal workforce?

       H10 . Psychological safety does not predict the employee engagement of United

States federal employees.

       H1α. Psychological safety predicts the employee engagement of United States

federal employees.

Research Question Two

       Does employee engagement predict psychological safety in the United States

federal workforce?

       H20. Employee engagement does not predict the psychological safety of United

States federal employees.

       H2α. Employee engagement predicts the psychological safety of United States

federal employees.

                                  Population and Sample

       The target population for the study included federal, civil service employees of

        Sample size. A sample size of 200 respondents was established for this research

based on the work of Field (2009) and Hair et al. (2010). Hair et al. (2010) described

sample size in a multiple regression analysis as “perhaps the single most influential

element under the control of the researcher” (p. 174). Field (2009) summarized the issue

of sample size in multiple regression by noting that the “simplest rule of thumb is that the

bigger the sample size, the better” (p. 222). In multiple regression, sample size impacts

both statistical power and generalizability of the analysis (Hair et al., 2010).

        Statistical power is a term referring to the probability that the multiple regression

results will find an existing effect (Field, 2009). The researcher imposed upon the study a

minimum statistical power of 0.80, which indicates that the multiple regression model

will detect a statistically significant coefficient of determination (R2 ) 80% of the time it

occurs (Hair et al., 2010). The number of predictor variables in the multiple regression

analysis and the level of significance chosen are also factors that are considered in

determining appropriate sample size (Field, 2009; Hair et al. 2010). At a significance

level with an alpha value of 5% (α = .05), statistical power of 0.80, and up to 20 predictor

variables, Field (2009) noted that “if you’re expecting a medium effect, then a sample

size of 200 will always suffice” (p. 223).

       Gaining a sufficiently large number of participants will increase the potential for

the applicability of the study results to a broader population (Gelo et al., 2008; Hair et al.

2010). Hair et al. (2010) stressed the importance of sample size and the number of

independent variables to the generalizability of results in multiple regression. The desired

sample size for generalizability is 15 to 20 respondents per predictor variable (Hair et al.,

                                                                                           62
2010, p. 175). Establishing a sample size of 200 participants for the research afforded the

researcher the opportunity to include at least 10 predictor and control variables while still

meeting the criteria identified by Hair et al. (2010) for generalizability.

Instrumentation

        An online method is appropriate for studies in which the researcher seeks to

collect results rapidly using a high-quality survey (Joyner et al., 2013). The survey

instrument for this study contained a total of 36 questions. Included in the study survey

was a set of six demographic questions which were used in subsequent data analysis as

        Employee Engagement Scale. The Employee Engagement Scale (Shuck et al.,

2016) has been demonstrated to exhibit both reliability and validity. As such, the scale

was used to measure employee engagement in the study. Written permission to use the

scale was obtained from Dr. Bradley Shuck (Appendix D).

        Containing 12 items (Appendix B), the Employee Engagement Scale measures

overall employee engagement and affords the researcher the opportunity to further break

down employee engagement scores to reflect the cognitive, emotional, and behavioral

dimensions of engagement. The response to each question is measured on a five-point

Likert-type scale with answer choices from one (Strongly Disagree) to five (Strongly

Agree). Sample items from the Employee Engagement Scale include “I am really focused

when I am working”; “I feel a strong sense of belonging to my job”; and “I am willing to

                                                                                           65
put in extra effort without being asked.” Shuck et al. (2016) grounded the theoretical

basis for their scale in the foundational work of Kahn (1990), further adding to the utility

of the instrument for measuring engagement in this study.

       During their work in developing the scale, Shuck et al. (2016) found internal

reliability scores, as measured by Cronbach’s alpha, of 0.88 for the emotional

engagement subscale, 0.91 for the behavioral scale, and 0.94 for the cognitive

engagement subscale (p. 16). According to Burns and Burns (2013) an internal reliability

value above 0.80, as measured using Cronbach’s alpha, is considered highly acceptable.

       To ensure the validity of the Employee Engagement Scale, Shuck et al. (2016)

examined the scale relative to factors that had previously been well supported within the

research as being positively correlated with employee engagement including “job

satisfaction, discretionary effort, and well being” (p. 16). Additionally, Shuck et al.

(2016) examined the scale relative to an employee’s “intent to turnover” (p. 16), which

was identified in the research as being negatively correlated with employee engagement.

In each case employee engagement, as measured by the Employee Engagement Scale and

the three subscales, was correlated with the factors that were well-described in the

research as being factors of employee engagement. As such, the Employee Engagement

Scale exhibited validity.

        Psychological safety scale. This study adopted the scale developed by

Edmondson (1999) for the measurement of psychological safety and slightly modified the

questions by changing generic question language describing the team to personalize the

scale to NSWC Crane. Written permission to use the scale was obtained from Dr. Amy

                                                                                             66
Edmondson (Appendix E). Edmondson’s scale includes seven items (Appendix B), each

of which are measured using a seven-point Likert-type scale with answer choices ranging

from one (Very Inaccurate) to seven (Very Accurate). Some of the items within

Edmondson’s (1999) psychological safety scale are reverse-scored and sample items

from the instrument include “If you make a mistake at NSWC Crane, it is often held

against you”; “It is safe to take a risk at NSWC Crane”; and “Working with members of

the NSWC Crane team, my unique skills and talents are valued and utilized.”

       Edmondson (1999) developed the psychological safety instrument in support of

the quantitative portion of a study designed to follow up on initial qualitative research.

Edmondson (1999) found that the psychological safety instrument exhibited internal

reliability of 0.82 (p. 363), which is above the value of 0.80 identified by Burns and

Burns (2013) as the threshold for highly acceptable reliability. The scale further

displayed validity as demonstrated by the development of a multi-trait multimethod

matrix that provided confirmation that variables designed to measure similar dimensions

were significantly more highly correlated than those that were not (Edmondson, 1999).

       Control variables. To ensure valid results, control variables were necessary in

designing this research study. Control variables are useful in cases where it is important

“to eliminate the influence of a third variable” (Burns & Burns, 2013, p. 361). In the

study, psychological meaningfulness and psychological availability were included in the

multiple regression analysis as control variables in alignment with Kahn’s (1990)

conceptualization of employee engagement in an effort to ensure that the results obtained

isolated the effect of psychological safety on employee engagement. Further, it was

thought to be necessary to hold certain demographic characteristics constant as each has

previously been demonstrated in the research literature to impact employee engagement.

       Psychological meaningfulness and psychological availability. As previously

noted, Kahn (1990) identified psychological meaningfulness, psychological safety, and

psychological availability as necessary conditions for employee engagement. In order to

ensure that the relationship between employee engagement and psychological safety was

properly characterized, the multiple regression analysis in this study controlled for

psychological meaningfulness and psychological availability. May et al. (2004)

developed instruments with which to measure psychological meaningfulness and

psychological availability that were leveraged in this research. The researcher secured

permission to use the scales from Dr. Douglas May (Appendix F).

       The psychological meaningfulness scale developed by May et al. (2004) consists

of six questions (Appendix B) measured on a five-point Likert-type scale with answer

choices from one (Strongly Disagree) to five (Strongly Agree). Sample items from the

psychological meaningfulness scale include “The work I do on this job is very important

to me”; “My job activities are very meaningful to me”; and “I feel that the work I do on

my job is valuable.” May et al. (2004) found that the psychological meaningfulness

instrument exhibited internal reliability of 0.90 (p. 21) which surpasses the level for

highly acceptable reliability (Burns & Burns, 2013).

       The psychological availability scale developed by May et al. (2004) consists of

five questions (Appendix B). The questions are measured on a five-point Likert-type

scale with answers ranging from one (Strongly Disagree) to five (Strongly Agree).

Sample questions from the psychological availability scale include “I am confident in my

ability to handle competing demands at work”; “I am confident in my ability to deal with

problems that come up at work”; and “I am confident that I can handle the physical

demands at work.” The psychological availability instrument exhibited internal

reliability of 0.85 (May et al., 2004, p. 21) which surpasses the level for highly acceptable

reliability (Burns & Burns, 2013).

        Gender. Pitt-Catsouphes and Matz-Costa (2008) found gender to be a factor in

employee engagement. Female employees were found to be more engaged than their

male colleagues (Pitt-Catsouphes & Matz-Costa, 2008). Therefore, the research

controlled for gender in an attempt to mitigate any potential influence on the relationship

between employee engagement and psychological safety.

        Employee age. Multiple researchers have suggested a relationship between age

and employee engagement (Pitt-Catsouphes & Matz-Costa, 2008; Rigg et al., 2014;

Simpson, 2008). The research results indicate that employee engagement increases as

employees age. Whereas the workforce of NSWC Crane spans several generations, it was

deemed important to control for the effects of age. Accordingly, employee age was a

control variable in this research.

        Supervisory status. In research related to federal employees, the United States

Merit System Protection Board (2008) found a significant relationship between

supervisory status and employee engagement. Supervisors were found to be more highly

engaged than non-supervisors. Accordingly, this study established supervisory status as a

control variable to eliminate the potential for undue influence on the data analysis.

        Tenure. Research related to an employee’s length of service (tenure) has provided

conflicting conclusions. Research results supporting a correlation between employee

engagement and tenure have been reported (Bal et al., 2013; Sibiya et al., 2014). On the

other hand, Rigg et al. (2014) found results indicating that the relationship between the

tenure and employee engagement was not correlated. This study adopted a conservative

approach and controlled for tenure to mitigate any affects that might manifest themselves

in the results.

        Telework. Sardeshmukh et al. (2012) reported a negative relationship between

employee engagement and telework. Within the NSWC Crane workforce, employees are

afforded the opportunity to telework in cases where permitted, based on the nature of the

job being performed. Often, NSWC Crane employees that telework will do so a few days

per week as opposed to full time. While the research of Sardeshmukh et al. (2012)

focused on full-time telework employees, this study adopted a conservative approach and

included telework as a control variable.

        Time of day. Sonnentag and Kuhnel (2016) found that employee engagement was

dependent on the time of day. Employees were found to be more engaged in the morning

than in the afternoon. This study controlled for the time of day by using the time stamp as

indicated in the Survey Monkey data for each respondent in order to determine whether

the subject responded to the survey in the morning or the afternoon. Each response was

coded accordingly and the variable was added into the multiple regression equation as a

control variable.

       The purpose of this non-experimental, quantitative study was to examine the

relationship between employee engagement and psychological safety within the federal

workforce. An online survey combined several instruments to measure variables used in

the study. Employee engagement was quantitatively measured using the Employee

Engagement Scale (Shuck et al., 2016) and psychological safety was quantified using the

scale developed by Edmondson (1999). Psychological meaningfulness and psychological

Survey Monkey website to indicate the time at which the survey was completed.

the study population will be presented, followed by a discussion of statistical assumptions

related to the study. The results will then be summarized and the findings associated with

hypothesis testing will be presented. The chapter will close with an assessment of the

                                 Statistical Assumptions

        In order to increase the likelihood that a predictive model developed using

multiple regression on a sample is indicative of results that might be gained from

examining the entire population, several assumptions about the data must be met (Field,

2009). In order to meet the assumptions of regression, the data was assessed for

independence of observations, linearity, homoscedasticity, multicollinearity, normally

distributed errors, and outliers (Burns & Burns, 2013; Field, 2009; Hair et al., 2010;

Laerd Statistics, 2015). The design of the study met necessary assumptions related to the

model containing a continuous dependent variable and two or more independent variables

(Laerd Statistics, 2015).

                                                                                            80
Independence of Observations

       Simply stated, the assumption of independence of observations means that each

data point of the outcome variable originates from a different source (Field, 2009). To

assess the data for independence of observations, the Durbin-Watson statistic was

calculated using SPSS and a result of 2.23 was obtained. The range of values for the

Durbin-Watson statistic is from 0 to 4. Durbin-Watson values approximating 2 indicate

independence (Field, 2009) and results between 1.5 and 2.5 provide support to indicate

that the data exhibits independence of observations (Rovai, Baker, & Ponton, 2014).

Based on the Durbin-Watson statistic obtained, the study was determined to have met the

assumption for independence of observations.

                             Statistical Power and Effect Size

       Cohen (1992) suggested that sample size, significance level, effect size, and

statistical power were interrelated in such a way “that each is a function of the other

three” (p. 156). Achieving statistical power in a study will assist the researcher in

increasing the likelihood that a null hypothesis will correctly be rejected (Burns & Burns,

2013). The effect size, on the other hand, affords the researcher the opportunity to

express the strength of a relationship identified through significance testing.

       As was noted in Chapter 3, the study sought to achieve a statistical power of 0.80,

which indicates that the regression model will detect a significant coefficient of

determination 80% of the time (Hair et al., 2010). The research design prescribed a

significance level of α = .05 and a sample size of 200 since a medium effect was

anticipated (Field, 2009). The study achieved an actual sample size of 210 and hypothesis

testing was completed at the α = .05 significance level, thereby achieving the desired

statistical power level of 0.80.

       The most recent (6th Ed.) of the Publication Manual of the American

Psychological Association (2013) notes “it is almost always necessary to include some

measure of the effect size in the Results section” (p. 34). Burns and Burns (2013)

suggested that a statistical analysis that produces a significant result does not explain the

magnitude of the effect between the independent and dependent variables. An

examination of the unstandardized coefficients within the regression results tables (Table

7 and Table 9) provides an explanation of effect size in terms of how much change in the

dependent variable can be attributed to a one unit change in each independent variable.

                                                                                                        94
While a review of unstandardized coefficients provides perspective on effect size

between variables, an additional measure, like that developed by Cohen (1992), can

provide a complementary analysis with which to provide evaluation of a relationship

between models (Burns & Burns, 2013, Field, 2009).

        The reporting of effect size can be useful in determining the practical importance

of statistically significant results. For multiple regression results, Cohen’s (1992) effect

size may be calculated using the formula f 2 = R2 / 1 – R2 , where R2 is the coefficient of

determination. Since this study used hierarchical regression, the additional explanatory

capability of the regression equation at each step becomes the effect size of interest. The

adaptation of Cohen’s (1992) formula for hierarchical regression would then be

expressed as f 2 = R2 (Step 2) – R2 (Step 1) / 1 – R2 (Step 2). Cohen (1992) categorized effect sizes

and identified .35 as the threshold for a large effect, .15 as a medium effect, and .02 to

designate a small effect.

        Effect sizes for first hypothesis. The first research question asked if

psychological safety was a predictor of employee engagement in the United States federal

workforce. The first hierarchical regression model contained the demographic control

variables and yielded a nonsignificant result with an R2 value of .052. The second

hierarchical regression model added psychological meaningfulness and psychological

availability to the first model. The second model yielded an increase in R2 of .564, which

means that the second model predicted 56.4% more of the variance in employee

engagement than the first model. Overall, the second model, which contained all of the

study control variables explained 61.6% of the variance in employee engagement level.

                                                                                          95
       In the third model of the hierarchical regression analysis, psychological safety

was added as the independent variable of interest. The third hierarchical regression model

amplified the overall predictive capability with an R2 increase of .011, which means that

the addition of psychological safety in the model increased predictive capability by 1.1%.

While the increase in R2 as a result of the addition of psychological safety was

determined significant during hypothesis testing, the calculated effect size of the increase

was .029, thereby indicating that the effect size on the increased predictive capability of

the full model is small (Cohen, 1992).

       Effect sizes for second hypothesis. The second research question asked if

Kahn’s (1990) results. Further, Wollard and Shuck (2011) noted an overall lack of

validation studies related to employee engagement. A key implication of this study is the

       Gender as a predictor of employee engagement. This study provides additional

support and depth to research related to gender’s impact on employee engagement. As

indicated in Chapter 2, Pitt-Catsouphes and Matz-Costa (2008) reported that female

employees were more highly engaged than their male counterparts. The results obtained

in the specification of the full model in this study revealed that gender was a statistically

significant variable. Female employees of NSWC Crane were found to be more highly

engaged than their male colleagues. Therefore, this study adds depth to the body of

research related to gender and employee engagement by providing additional support for

the results obtained by Pitt-Catsouphes and Matz-Costa (2008).

        Employee engagement as a predictor of psychological safety. Christian et al.

(2011) proposed that increased employee engagement may lead to a reciprocal condition

whereby measures of certain antecedents of employee engagement may increase. This

study examined the reciprocal relationship in which psychological safety, an antecedent

of employee engagement, may increase in the presence of increased employee

engagement. The theoretical implications of this study are that employee engagement

                                                                                           102
levels were statistically significant in predicting psychological safety, thereby building on

the work of Christian et al. (2011) to characterize a specific reciprocal relationship.

Frazier et al. (2017) identified the potential reciprocal relationship by which employee

engagement predicts psychological safety as an important, yet unanswered, question.

This study provides an initial answer, thereby addressing a gap in the literature.

       Social exchange theory. Saks (2006) asserted that a social exchange relationship

in the workplace can serve to promote increased levels of employee engagement. Saks

(2006) further provided a theoretical basis for the reciprocal condition whereby employee

engagement impacts psychological safety as a result of the social exchange that occurs

when the employee is engaged in a high-quality relationship with his or her supervisor.

Since a high-quality relationship with the supervisor is an antecedent of psychological

safety (Edmondson, 1999; Kahn, 1990), an important theoretical implication of this study

is that the results showing employee engagement as a statistically significant predictor of

psychological safety provide an extension of Saks’ (2006) work and offer support for the

application of social exchange theory to describe the relationship between employee

engagement and psychological safety.

       Engagement in the public sector. An important theoretical implication of this

study is inherent with the subject matter of the research. Jin and McDonald (2016)

suggested that employee engagement in the federal workforce is a topic that remains

under-researched. The operation of the federal sector enterprise is different than that of

the private sector (Seidle et al., 2016). This study adds to the scholarly research by

                                                                                           103
providing results related to employee engagement and psychological safety within an

under-researched sector.

         Employee Engagement Scale. Support for the Employee Engagement Scale

(Shuck et al., 2016) as a viable and effective measure of employee engagement is an

important theoretical implication of this study. The use of the scale as a measure of

employee engagement adds to the body of research related to the measurement of

employee engagement. Additionally, to the best of the researcher’s knowledge, the

deployment of the Employee Engagement Scale for this study marks the first application

within the federal sector.

         Correlations. Employee engagement and psychological safety share several

antecedents (Chughtai & Buckley, 2011; Dollard & Bakker, 2010; Edmondson, 1999;

Wang & Hsieh, 2013). While sharing similarities in antecedents, employee engagement

and psychological safety are separate constructs (Frazier et al., 2017; Khoreva & van

Zalk, 2016; Shuck, Adelson, & Reio, 2016). The moderate correlation between the two

constructs (.43) as displayed in Table 4 implies support for the argument that the

constructs are separate and adds to the body of knowledge on the subject. It should be

noted that Table 4 also indicates a strong correlation between psychological

meaningfulness and employee engagement within the data (.75), thereby providing

additional theoretical implications by which this study supports results reported in the

literature (Kahn, 1990; May et al., 2004; Rich et al., 2011; Shuck, Rocco, and Albornoz,

2011).

                                                                                          104
        Demographics. The inclusion of demographic control variables related to time of

day, gender, supervisory status, telework participation, tenure, and age within the

hierarchical regression was a conservative approach to study design based on scant, and

sometimes conflicting, prior research results on the impact of each demographic variable

on employee engagement. The goal of the study design was to limit the potential

confounding effects of the aforementioned variables in an attempt to isolate and describe

the effect of the independent variable of interest. A theoretical implication of this study

was that, other than gender, none of the demographic variables were statistically

significant predictors of employee engagement. Relative to the prediction of

psychological safety, none of the demographic control variables were statistically

significant predictors.

Practical Implications

        Rivera and Flinck (2011) estimated that the lack of productivity as a result of

disengaged federal employees cost the United States taxpayers $65 billion annually (p.

487). An overarching practical implication of this study is that a greater understanding of

the relationship between employee engagement and psychological safety can be gained

through a review of the results. When leveraged by federal management practitioners

seeking to maximize employee engagement (Babcock-Roberson & Strickland, 2010;

Chandani et al., 2016; Harter et al., 2002; Saks, 2006; Shuck & Wollard, 2010) and

minimize costs associated with disengaged employees Bakker, 2011; Shuck & Reio,

2014; Christian et al., 2011) , the results of this study may provide impetus to an effective

strategic approach, thereby filling a gap in the literature related to the employee

                                                                                         105
engagement field of research in general (Wollard & Shuck, 2011) and specifically related

to the federal sector (Rivera & Flinck, 2011).

       Social exchange applied in the federal workplace. While the study provided the

aforementioned theoretical implications related to social exchange theory, practical

implications for the federal practitioner are also indicated. The reciprocal nature of the

relationship between employee engagement and psychological safety found in the study

results can be instructive for the federal manager. Homans (1958) described practical

implications of social exchange within the organization in suggesting that workplace

conditions that affirm employees and provide favorable sentiment produce an

environment in which employees tend to offer affirmative behavior in exchange. From a

practitioner perspective, the application of the study results indicates that when

conditions are created in which employees feel psychologically safe, they can reasonably

be expected to exhibit increased levels of employee engagement and vice versa. The

researcher asserts that employee engagement and psychological safety are not behaviors

that can be demanded of the employee by the management practitioner. As noted in

Chapter 2, poorly executed managerial efforts at increasing employee engagement can

result in negative results and employee stress (Jenkins & Delbridge, 2013). Accordingly,

an approach designed to manipulate the work environment or create a sense of obligation

on the part of the employee to become engaged or to feel psychologically safe is not

appropriate. Rather, it is advised that the federal management practitioner seeking to

increase employee engagement and psychological safety understand the importance of

                                                                                          106
creating conducive workplace conditions that create a favorable social exchange for both

parties, thus creating reciprocity.

        Measurement of employee engagement. It is important that management

practitioners regularly measure and track employee engagement within the workforce. In

doing so, the effectiveness of strategic efforts to increase employee engagement can be

monitored and evaluated. A practical implication of this study is the ease with which an

instrument, such as the Employee Engagement Scale (Shuck et al., 2016) may be

leveraged in the regular measurement of employee engagement.

        Research-backed guidance. There remains a need for research-based guidance

on the topics of employee engagement and psychological safety (Shuck & Herd, 2012;

Shuck & Reio, 2011; Welch, 2011). A practical implication of this study is that

actionable insight is provided relative to employee engagement and psychological safety.

The savvy practitioner can benefit from understanding the reciprocal nature of the

relationship between the two constructs as identified in this study. The greater awareness

can subsequently be manifest through the adoption of a strategic approach characterized

by a dual-focus to maximize both employee engagement and psychological safety. In

such a manner, organizational benefits can accrue.

        Small effect size. The discussion of practical implications must necessarily be

tempered with a reminder of the effect sizes associated with the mutual predictive

capabilities of employee engagement and psychological safety. While statistically

significant in this study, effect sizes for the prediction of employee engagement based on

psychological safety and vice versa were classified as small (Cohen, 1992). The practical

                                                                                          107
implication of the small effect size is that practitioner efforts to increase employee

engagement through psychological safety and vice versa must be part of an overall

comprehensive strategic approach, as opposed to standalone strategies.

                                         Limitations

       There are several limitations associated with this study. The study limitations

include, but are not limited to, those associated with the nature of quantitative

methodology, online survey, statistical limitations, and effect size. This section will

provide a brief explanation of each.

       The quantitative methodology is useful in the examination of relationships

between variables (Hair et al., 2010; Joyner et al., 2013) and for cases where the

researcher wishes to predict the value of a variable based on a combination of other

variables (Burns & Burns, 2013; Field, 2009; Joyner et al., 2013). The nature of the

quantitative methodology is such that the question as to why a relationship between

variables may exist is not explained. Qualitative research, on the other hand, provides a

platform with which the researcher can investigate relationships between variables in-

depth to better understand why the relationship exists. A potential limitation of this

research, therefore, is the focus on strictly the quantitative methodology, instead of

incorporating a qualitative component and conducting a mixed-methods study.

       The online nature of this study provided an anonymous platform for participants

to respond to the survey. Several potential limitations are inherent with online surveys.

While every effort was made to provide concise and clear instructions, it is conceivable

that participants did not understand some aspect of the survey, which could have been

                                                                                            108
reflected by inaccurate responses. Since exclusion criteria were applied to the sample,

another potential limitation with the online survey is that the veracity of user responses to

demographic and qualifier questions cannot be assured. It is possible that users may have

misrepresented themselves while taking the survey. Further, since some of the items

within the survey instrument were reverse-coded, the potential for the participants to have

misunderstood the scale and incorrectly answered reverse questions is a potential

limitation to the study.

        The study met the minimum sample size of 200 as specified by Hair et al. (2010)

for generalizability of study results. All study participants were civilian employees of

NSWC Crane. A potential limitation of this study is that the culture within the federal

government organization is not analogous to that of private industry (Lavigna, 2011;

Seidle, 2016; Trahant, 2009), thereby forcing one to exercise caution when applying the

study results to a broader population beyond the workforce of the federal government. An

additional potential limitation related to statistical analysis associated with the study is

that, although the regression analysis provided statistically significant results to indicate

relationships between employee engagement and psychological safety, causation cannot

be assumed (Sedgwick, 2014).

        The rather small effect size, as calculated using the formula specified by Cohen

(1992), provides yet another potential limitation to this study. The calculated effect of

psychological safety on employee engagement and vice versa was small according to

Cohen’s (1992) classification. Despite statistically significant relationships between the

                                                                                          109
two, the small effect sizes provide a potential limitation to the study when one attempts to

interpret and apply the results.

                           Recommendations for Future Research

        Several recommendations for future research may be made based on the findings

of this study. First, a replication of this study is recommended. A replication of the study

would ostensibly provide validation of the statistically significant relationships between

employee engagement and psychological safety found in this study. Within the

suggestion of a replication study, it is further recommended that the variables be more

fully examined through the use of structural equation modeling. The application of

structural equation modeling would afford the researcher the opportunity to examine all

of the variables simultaneously, as opposed to using a series of blocks, as is inherent in

hierarchical regression.

        A further explanation of the relationship between psychological meaningfulness,

one of the control variables in the study, and employee engagement is recommended. The

researcher theorizes that the study population, employees of NSWC Crane, is engaged in

work that is deemed to be personally meaningful. The close correlation (.75) between

employee engagement and psychological meaningfulness within the study population

may be unique and reflective of the nature of the work being performed at NSWC Crane

or the federal workforce at large. Trahant (2009) described the sense of altruism

associated with the perspective of federal employees related to their work. Therefore, a

close examination of psychological meaningfulness related to employee engagement,

                                                                                           110
comparing results from within the federal sector with other industry segments would

further extend the literature on the topic.

        A follow-up qualitative study to further explore the reciprocal nature of the

relationship between employee engagement and psychological safety in the federal

workforce is recommended. Through the use of the qualitative tradition, the research can

further explore the nature of the relationship between the constructs and, perhaps, more

fully explain why the relationship exists. Such research could potentially be further

expanded to examine psychological meaningfulness and psychological availability to

explore potential reciprocal relationships, collectively and individually, with employee

engagement.

        The use of the Employee Engagement Scale (Shuck et al., 2016) in this study

presents a recommendation for future research. In their treatise describing the scale,

Shuck et al. (2016) suggest that the Employee Engagement Scale be used alongside other

engagement scales for the purpose of side-by-side comparison. This study was

conceptually grounded in the original work on employee engagement performed by Kahn

(1990) and the Employee Engagement Scale was chosen for this study due to the fact that

Shuck et al. (2016) also grounded their development of the scale in the work of Kahn

(1990). A recommendation for future research is to utilize the ISA Engagement Scale

(Soane et al., 2012), which was also conceptually based on the foundational work of

Kahn (1990), to perform a side-by-side comparison of the scales.

        A final recommendation is based on the study results related to the reciprocal

relationship in which employee engagement predicts psychological safety. Frazier et al

                                                                                          111
(2017) identified this as an unanswered question. It is recommended that further research,

based on the premise of Christian et al. (2011) that increased employee engagement

enhances some of the antecedents of employee engagement be conducted. In addition to

psychological safety, other known antecedents to employee engagement could be

examined as part of this recommendation for future research.

                                        Conclusions

       The intent of this study was to examine the relationship between employee

engagement and psychological safety. The study was guided by two research questions

(a) Does psychological safety predict the employee engagement of United States federal

employees?, and (b) Does employee engagement predict the psychological safety of

United States federal employees? Chapter 1 provided an introduction to the study.

Chapter 2 offered a review of the literature related to the employee engagement and

psychological safety. Chapter 3 presented the research design and methodology. Chapter

4 outlined the analysis of the data and results. Chapter 5 focused on a discussion of the

results, implications, limitations, and suggestions for future research.

       Two important findings from the study were reported. First, psychological safety

is a statistically significant predictor of employee engagement. As one’s level of

psychological safety increases, so does one’s level of employee engagement. Further

examination revealed that the effect size of the predictive capability of psychological

safety on employee engagement is small. Next, employee engagement is a statistically

significant predictor of psychological safety. When increased employee engagement is

experienced, psychological safety is increased. Thus, employee engagement and

                                                                                        112
psychological safety can be said to act as reciprocal predictors. As with the previous case,

subsequent examination revealed that the effect size for employee engagement on

psychological safety is small. Despite the small effect size, federal managers can use the

results of this study to formulate strategies with which to enhance organizational

performance through increased levels of employee engagement and psychological safety.

I am really focused when I am working
I concentrate on my job when I am at work
I give my job responsibility a lot of attention

Working at NSWC Crane has a great deal of personal meaning to me
I feel a strong sense of belonging to my job
I believe in the mission and purpose of NSWC Crane
I care about the future of NSWC Crane
I really push myself to work beyond what is expected of me
I am willing to put in extra effort without being asked
I often go above what is expected of me to help my team be successful
I work harder than expected to help NSWC Crane be successful

Psychological Safety, 7-point scale (Edmonson, 1999)
If you make a mistake at NSWC Crane, it is often held against you
Members of the NSWC Crane team are able to bring up problems and tough issues
People at NSWC Crane sometimes reject others for being different
It is safe to take a risk at NSWC Crane
It is difficult to ask other members of the NSWC Crane team for help
No one at NSWC Crane would deliberately act in a way that undermines my efforts
Working with members of the NSWC Crane team, my unique skills and talents are
valued and utilized

Psychological Meaningfulness, 5-point scale (May et al., 2004)
The work I do on this job is very important to me
My job activities are personally meaningful to me
The work I do on this job is worthwhile
My job activities are significant to me
The work I do on this job is meaningful to me
I feel that the work I do on my job is valuable

Psychological Availability, 5-point scale (May et al., 2004)
I am confident in my ability to handle competing demands at work
I am confident in my ability to deal with problems that come up at work
I am confident in my ability to think clearly at work
I am confident in my ability to display the appropriate emotions at work
I am confident that I can handle the physical demands at work
